# Hungary has new evidence in case involving Ukrainian cash couriers

Hungary’s National Tax and Customs Administration says it has “new evidence” in a case involving the seizure of Ukrainian cash-in-transit vehicles and the confiscation of cash and gold on March 5, [government spokesman Zoltan Kovacs said](https://x.com/zoltanspox/status/2041770966183825712?s=20&fbclid=IwY2xjawRC949leHRuA2FlbQIxMABicmlkETF6WVRoQU5VRDJYWjF0dFVEc3J0YwZhcHBfaWQQMjIyMDM5MTc4ODIwMDg5MgABHnhVnNHlTjds0TTlxYmdoyT_YwTmmN4vNK2DnSmXLjOb3NrWtJxerglkk5He_aem_PwwFumsUyyw-kADp0zyKrQ)on Apr. 8.

Kovacs said the armored vehicles were allegedly transporting “freshly printed euros and dollars.” He claimed the banknotes had never entered circulation.

According to him, they were “linked to a number of banks, including Ukraine’s Oschadbank, as well as financial institutions in Poland and Gibraltar.”

“Among the evidence is a video allegedly showing a former major general of Ukrainian intelligence falsifying documents in a gas station restroom while his associates discuss corruption-related payments. The cargo amounted to about $40 million, €35 million and 9 kilograms of gold, the origin and purpose of which remain unclear,” a Hungarian government representative said.

He added that Hungary “requested international cooperation and rejected allegations of wrongdoing, stressing that all investigative actions were documented and carried out in accordance with the law.”

To substantiate the alleged offense, Hungary presented a video showing Ukrainian national Hennadii Kuznetsov, who had previously been detained and worked in the security services, allegedly falsifying documents in a gas station restroom while his colleagues discuss “corruption money.”

The outlet Telex contacted Oschadbank’s lawyer, who said suspicions about the origin of the banknotes “do not even provide grounds for a basic suspicion of money laundering, as all these transactions are international financial operations.”

At the same time, the lawyer stressed that the video featuring the Ukrainian citizen is old — it was recorded in 2023 and has no connection to the case.

“Without specifying the place, time and participants, the video is nothing more than a distraction for the investigative body, unsuitable for establishing criminal liability or even for notifying a basic suspicion,” the lawyer said.

He also emphasized that the defense has not yet received substantial evidence. The prosecution has also failed to answer questions about “the legal basis on which the Hungarian state is holding the assets, and what justified the unlawful detention, expulsion and inhumane treatment of the money transport personnel.”

**Detention of Oschadbank cash collection staff in Hungary**

On the night of March 6, it became known that Hungary had detained two armored vehicles of Oschadbank’s cash collection service and seven Ukrainian citizens — members of the cash collection team — during the transit transport of valuables through Budapest.

The vehicles were carrying $40 million, €35 million and 9 kilograms of gold. Ukraine’s Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha said Hungarian authorities had effectively taken Ukrainians hostage and seized the money.

Later, Hungary’s National Tax and Customs Administration said the seven Oschadbank employees were detained on suspicion of money laundering.

Ukraine’s National Police opened criminal proceedings over the alleged abduction of Ukrainian citizens and the seizure of an Oschadbank service vehicle.

On the evening of March 6, the seven Oschadbank cash collection employees who had been unlawfully detained in Hungary during a service trip returned to Ukraine. According to National Bank of Ukraine Governor Andrii Pyshnyi, one of them was hospitalized after being interrogated by Hungarian authorities.

On March 12, Oschadbank’s press service said Hungary had returned the armored vehicles, but they were damaged. The cash and gold remain under arrest in Hungary.
