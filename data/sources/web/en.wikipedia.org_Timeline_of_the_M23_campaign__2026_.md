This **timeline of the** **[M23 campaign (2022–present)](/wiki/M23_campaign_(2022%E2%80%93present) "M23 campaign (2022–present)")** covers the period from January 2026 to the present day.

January 2026
------------

### 1–4 January

On 1 January 2026, M23 rebels from Kasopo, [Masisi Territory](/wiki/Masisi_Territory "Masisi Territory"), attempted to outflank FARDC positions near Buhimba on the [Walikale](/wiki/Walikale_Territory "Walikale Territory")–[Masisi](/wiki/Masisi_Territory "Masisi Territory") border but were repelled by a joint FARDC–Wazalendo force and retreated back to Kasopo and Mahanga as FARDC reportedly captured around ten rebels.[[1]](#cite_note-1)[[2]](#cite_note-2) On 2 January, M23 clashed with FARDC–Wazalendo forces in Kasenyi, in the Katoyi sector of Masisi Territory, as part of an effort to "expand their area of influence".[[3]](#cite_note-3) On 3 January, they launched another attack in Kilongo, about 3 km from Buhimba in the Waloa Yungu *[groupement](/wiki/Chiefdoms_and_sectors_of_the_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Chiefdoms and sectors of the Democratic Republic of the Congo")* of Walikale Territory.[[4]](#cite_note-4) In Masisi Territory, M23 clashed with FARDC–Wazalendo forces in Bingaro, Katoyi, after reinforcing from Kazinga, while another rebel advance toward Humura was repelled by "loyalist forces" backed by Wazalendo.[[5]](#cite_note-5) That same day, FARDC presented captured suspects in Kinshasa, including seven Rwandan soldiers and eight foreign civilians, as evidence of Rwanda's direct involvement alongside M23 in [North](/wiki/North_Kivu "North Kivu") and [South Kivu](/wiki/South_Kivu "South Kivu"), arguing that the arrests contradicted Rwanda's claims of a defensive posture, demonstrated that it was actively waging war inside Congolese territory, attempting to regionalize the conflict by involving foreign nationals and allied armed groups, and violating [UN Security Council](/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council") resolutions as well as the [December 2025 Washington peace agreement](/wiki/2025_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo%E2%80%93Rwanda_peace_agreement "2025 Democratic Republic of the Congo–Rwanda peace agreement").[[6]](#cite_note-6)

### 5–11 January

On 5 January, President Tshisekedi met Angolan President [João Lourenço](/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Louren%C3%A7o "João Lourenço") in [Luanda](/wiki/Luanda "Luanda"), during which Tshisekedi said Lourenço presented "interesting" ideas aimed at resolving the conflict in eastern DRC.[[7]](#cite_note-:0-7) A follow-up meeting scheduled for 8 January was intended to further develop these proposals, whose specific details remain unclear, and to underline that any Angolan-led initiative would not replace the Washington and [Doha](/wiki/Doha "Doha") tracks but rather encourage the parties to honor their existing commitments, with Lourenço potentially facilitating political dialogue in Kinshasa involving Tshisekedi and various Congolese stakeholders, including M23, opposition figures, and representatives of religious and civil society groups, who would participate without leading the process.[[7]](#cite_note-:0-7)[[8]](#cite_note-8) Although the leaders did not address calls for a fully inclusive dialogue sought by some Congolese elites, Angola reportedly reached out to certain opposition members about possible talks. Tshisekedi stated that any engagement would first require M23 to announce an immediate and unconditional ceasefire, which he would then assess.[[7]](#cite_note-:0-7)

Meanwhile, violence continued on the ground: between 5 and 8 January, four people were killed in [Sake](/wiki/Sake,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Sake, Democratic Republic of the Congo") by armed men believed to be M23 rebels who control the area,[[9]](#cite_note-9) and on 6 January, the group dropped bombs in Katobo forest in Walikale Territory's Kisimba *groupement*, which alarmed residents and farmers despite no active clashes in the area.[[10]](#cite_note-10) On 7 January, the M23 reinforced its positions in Mpeti, Kisimba, with three trucks carrying fighters and ammunition from Kalembe, 18 km from [Pinga](/wiki/Pinga,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Pinga, Democratic Republic of the Congo"), before returning empty, as local officials stated that the buildup aimed to launch long-planned offensives against the strategic town of Pinga.[[11]](#cite_note-11) On 11 January, Wazalendo attacked M23 positions in Kazinga, using heavy and light weapons to preempt a rebel offensive against FARDC–Wazalendo positions in the Nyamaboko 1 and 2 *groupements*.[[12]](#cite_note-12) In [Fizi Territory](/wiki/Fizi_Territory "Fizi Territory"), a vessel known as *Super Bus*, en route to [Bujumbura](/wiki/Bujumbura "Bujumbura") for repairs while navigating [Lake Tanganyika](/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika "Lake Tanganyika"), was intercepted near Makobola 2 by a joint FARDC maritime and Wazalendo patrol. This followed the seizure of another boat, *Okako 2*, captured on 8 January in the village of Mizimu, also in Fizi Territory.[[13]](#cite_note-:1-13) Since [M23's capture of Uvira](/wiki/2025_Uvira_offensive "2025 Uvira offensive"), authorities imposed additional restrictions to curb movement in the region, including a ban on traffic between South Kivu and [Tanganyika](/wiki/Tanganyika_Province "Tanganyika Province") provinces via Lake Tanganyika, with all vessels now considered suspicious amid ongoing hostilities.[[13]](#cite_note-:1-13)

### 12–16 January

On 12 January, nine M23 fighters from [Lubero Territory](/wiki/Lubero_Territory "Lubero Territory") surrendered to the Wazalendo faction of the *Forces Patriotiques pour la Paix/Armée du Peuple* (FPP/AP) in Fatua in the Kisimba *groupement*, Walikale Territory, after "fleeing the suffering they endured in the rebellion".[[14]](#cite_note-14) On 15 January, M23 pushed through multiple locations in South Kivu, including [Kaziba Chiefdom](/wiki/Kaziba_Chiefdom "Kaziba Chiefdom"), Langala, and Rubanga, before reaching [Lemera](/wiki/Lemera "Lemera"), where its fighters split into two groups, one advancing toward [Kidote](/wiki/Kidote "Kidote") and the other toward Nakandongo.[[15]](#cite_note-15) That day, the movement announced that it was transferring responsibility for Uvira's security to the international community, reiterating its decision, first stated on 15 December 2025, to withdraw its forces. [Corneille Nangaa](/wiki/Corneille_Nangaa "Corneille Nangaa") stated that M23 had completely pulled out its monitoring unit and would no longer be responsible for maintaining security in Uvira, calling on international actors to protect civilians and uphold order.[[16]](#cite_note-:2-16) He warned that armed groups outside the peace process, including the [Burundian army](/wiki/Burundi_National_Defence_Force "Burundi National Defence Force"), the [FDLR](/wiki/Democratic_Forces_for_the_Liberation_of_Rwanda "Democratic Forces for the Liberation of Rwanda"), Wazalendo, and mercenaries allied with the Congolese army, could destabilize the city and undermine peace initiatives.[[16]](#cite_note-:2-16) Nonetheless, in Uvira, M23 rebels confiscated road [construction materials](/wiki/List_of_building_materials "List of building materials"), loading [tar](/wiki/Tar "Tar") onto 34 Wowo trucks bound for Rwanda, seizing [tricycles](/wiki/Tricycle "Tricycle") transported by other vehicles, evacuating remaining equipment through the [Kamanyola](/wiki/Kamanyola "Kamanyola") border crossing, and driving cattle and small livestock from the highlands and midlands toward [Bwegera](/wiki/Bwegera "Bwegera") to unknown destinations.[[17]](#cite_note-17) In Fizi Territory, M23-allied Twirwaneho rebels clashed with FARDC in Tuwetuwe, about 15 km from the Mikenge displacement site in the [Itombwe sector](/wiki/Mwenga_Territory#Administration_and_governance "Mwenga Territory") of [Mwenga Territory](/wiki/Mwenga_Territory "Mwenga Territory"), forcing government troops to retreat toward Point Zero village on the Fizi–Mwenga border in the [Mitumba](/wiki/Mitumba_Mountains "Mitumba Mountains") highlands, a settlement established in 2018 by [internally displaced persons](/wiki/Internally_displaced_person "Internally displaced person") fleeing violence in the highlands of Fizi, Mwenga, and [Uvira](/wiki/Uvira_Territory "Uvira Territory") Territories and serving as the FARDC's final defensive position.[[18]](#cite_note-:3-18) On 16 January, Twirwaneho rebels seized Point Zero and forced FARDC units to retreat to Mulima village in the [Basilotcha *groupement*](/wiki/Tanganyika,_Fizi_Territory#Governance_and_administrative_divisions "Tanganyika, Fizi Territory") of the [Tanganyika sector](/wiki/Tanganyika,_Fizi_Territory "Tanganyika, Fizi Territory"). Its capture opened a corridor for the rebels along the Mulima–Mukera axis toward Fizi-center, from where they could easily advance toward [Tanganyika Province](/wiki/Tanganyika_Province "Tanganyika Province") via Misisi without passing through [Baraka](/wiki/Baraka,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Baraka, Democratic Republic of the Congo"). After the fall of Point Zero, displaced civilians were once again forced to flee in heavy rains to Bilende, Kanguli, Nakiele, Mulima, and Lusuku, where they faced extremely precarious living conditions due to the lack of assistance after abandoning all their belongings.[[18]](#cite_note-:3-18)

### 16–20 January

Following a high-level meeting in [Lomé](/wiki/Lom%C3%A9 "Lomé") on 16–17 January, the [African Union](/wiki/African_Union "African Union") outlined a structured mediation framework for eastern DRC led by [Faure Gnassingbé](/wiki/Faure_Gnassingb%C3%A9 "Faure Gnassingbé") as chief mediator, and to address the conflict's complexity, the AU appointed five former African heads of state as co-facilitators: [Oluṣẹgun Ọbasanjọ](/wiki/Olusegun_Obasanjo "Olusegun Obasanjo") (military and security), [Sahle-Work Zewde](/wiki/Sahle-Work_Zewde "Sahle-Work Zewde") (humanitarian affairs), [Uhuru Kenyatta](/wiki/Uhuru_Kenyatta "Uhuru Kenyatta") (armed-group dialogue), [Mokgweetsi Masisi](/wiki/Mokgweetsi_Masisi "Mokgweetsi Masisi") (regional economic cooperation), and [Catherine Samba-Panza](/wiki/Catherine_Samba-Panza "Catherine Samba-Panza") (civil society, reconciliation, and women's issues). The framework also includes an independent joint secretariat bringing together the AU, regional organizations, and neighboring blocs, with the AU Commission coordinating with international partners such as the UN, Qatar, the EU, and major global powers.[[19]](#cite_note-19) The meeting urged both the DRC and M23 to fast-track the fulfillment of their commitments, emphasized peaceful solutions to the conflict, reiterated its full backing of the Doha process, and called on the parties to resume talks promptly to finalize negotiations on the six remaining protocols.[[20]](#cite_note-20) On 17 January, M23 stated that it had finalized its withdrawal from Uvira. While the bulk of its forces and equipment had departed, observers reported a residual presence, including a column of at least 200 armed fighters leaving the city, some walking and others transported by trucks, with heavy weapons visibly carried. Gunfire, including [RPG](/wiki/Rocket-propelled_grenade "Rocket-propelled grenade") fire, was reported around Uvira that day.[[21]](#cite_note-21) South Kivu Governor Jean-Jacques Purusi Sadiki said that M23 elements were still present and holding positions on hills overlooking the city, particularly in Katongo, Kigongo, [Makobola 1](/wiki/Makobola_1 "Makobola 1") and 2, and the Kivovo mountains. He also noted that other fighters had withdrawn toward [Sange](/wiki/Sange,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Sange, Democratic Republic of the Congo"), [Luvungi](/wiki/Luvungi "Luvungi"), and the [South Kivu Sugar Refinery](/wiki/South_Kivu_Sugar_Refinery "South Kivu Sugar Refinery"), roughly 15 kilometers from Uvira, calling the situation a clear breach of the ceasefire, which stipulates a withdrawal beyond [Kamanyola](/wiki/Kamanyola "Kamanyola"), outside Uvira Territory. On 18 January, witnesses reported the presence of youths claiming to be Wazalendo in certain *[quartiers](/wiki/Uvira#Administrative_history_and_divisions "Uvira")*, while FARDC troops were not reported in the city.[[22]](#cite_note-22) FARDC later confirmed on 19 January that it had reestablished control over Uvira on 18 January and stated that its forces remained deployed in and around the city to consolidate positions and protect civilians and their property.[[23]](#cite_note-23) Meanwhile, in Walikale Territory, FARDC carried out airstrikes on M23 positions on Mount Kasopo along the Kisimba–Ikobo border.[[24]](#cite_note-24)

### 21–29 January

On 21 January, Muhindo Mungumwa Claude, the M23-installed chief of [Kanyabayonga](/wiki/Kanyabayonga "Kanyabayonga") *groupement* in [Bwito Chiefdom](/wiki/Bwito_Chiefdom "Bwito Chiefdom"), and his wife were shot dead in their home by unidentified gunmen, which mirrors a similar October 2025 killing in Buramba where another chief, known as Maliro, was murdered with bladed weapons.[[25]](#cite_note-25) On 22 January, Rwanda for the first time openly acknowledged coordinating with M23 in a statement to the [U.S. Congress](/wiki/United_States_Congress "United States Congress"), in which Ambassador [Mathilde Mukantabana](/wiki/Mathilde_Mukantabana "Mathilde Mukantabana") said that Rwanda was "engaging in security coordination with the AFC/M23 in order to build trust through transparency", describing the cooperation as defensive, aimed at countering the FDLR and protecting Tutsi populations, and arguing that it helped persuade M23 to unilaterally withdraw from Uvira as evidence of de-escalation.[[26]](#cite_note-26)[[27]](#cite_note-27) The admission marked a major shift from Rwanda's longstanding denials of ties to M23 and prompted a Congolese national deputy, Joseph Nkoy Wembo, to declare that "the masks have fallen", as Rwanda acknowledged its direct role in the conflict in the DRC.[[28]](#cite_note-28) On 23 January, FARDC conducted attack-drone airstrikes that destroyed an M23 position in [Buleusa](/wiki/Buleusa "Buleusa"), in the Ikobo *groupement* of Walikale Territory, which forced the rebels to flee as the army increasingly relied on drone warfare to locate and neutralize rebel bases,[[29]](#cite_note-29) before targeting a new M23 position in Kikuku in Bwito Chiefdom, where residents reported significant material damage, including the destruction of the rebel-occupied camp.[[30]](#cite_note-30) That same day, M23 repeatedly attempted to enter [Pinga](/wiki/Pinga,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Pinga, Democratic Republic of the Congo") and outflank FARDC and Volunteers for the Defense of the Homeland (*Volontaires pour la Défense de la Patrie*; VDP) positions at the strategic Nkobe hill overlooking Munsanga village, but were repelled by loyalist forces and pushed back toward Buhaya near Mpeti.[[31]](#cite_note-31) On 24 January, FARDC drones bombed M23 positions in Mpeti and Mindjendje in Walikale Territory's Kisimba *groupement*, locations that are regarded as key regrouping and supply points for the rebels, who had been reinforced by units from [Kitshanga](/wiki/Kitchanga "Kitchanga"), Mwesso, and Kalembe in Masisi Territory.[[32]](#cite_note-32) On 26 January, two people were abducted by armed men in Kateku, Ikobo *groupement*, with the incident occurring in an M23-controlled area where recurrent kidnappings and looting in Walikale Territory have fueled local suspicions;[[33]](#cite_note-33) meanwhile, during the night of 25–26 January, the chief of Bushani village in the Luberike *groupement* was assassinated at his home under unclear circumstances. According to the administrative authorities of the Wanianga sector, the killing was attributed to M23 rebels active in the area.[[34]](#cite_note-34) On 29 January, M23 fighters advancing from Mpeti launched an attack on FARDC-Wazalendo positions and the Armed Reserve of the Defense (*Réserve armée de la défense*; RAD) around Chanjikiro, in the Kisimba *groupement* of Walikale Territory, but the attack was repelled by FARDC and its allied forces, and forced M23 units to withdraw toward Mpeti,[[35]](#cite_note-35) after which the [Congolese Air Force](/wiki/Air_Force_of_the_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Air Force of the Democratic Republic of the Congo") carried out drone strikes on rebel positions in Mindjendje near Mpeti.[[36]](#cite_note-36) According to Major Dieudonné Kasereka, spokesperson for the FARDC's [34th Military Region](/wiki/Land_Forces_of_the_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo#Updates_in_2014 "Land Forces of the Democratic Republic of the Congo"), a total of 265 M23 rebels surrendered to the FARDC in January 2026, primarily in Walikale, Rutshuru, and Lubero Territories in North Kivu, as well as [Kalehe Territory](/wiki/Kalehe_Territory "Kalehe Territory") in South Kivu.[[37]](#cite_note-37)

Late January–February 2026
--------------------------

### 29 January–8 February

In Fizi Territory, Wazalendo launched an offensive to capture [Minembwe](/wiki/Minembwe "Minembwe"). A FARDC attack drone reportedly bombed the Madegu *quartier* in Minembwe around 31 January. Between 31 January and 2 February, FARDC-Wazalendo forces reportedly engaged in fighting with Twirwaneho and [RED-Tabara](/wiki/RED-Tabara "RED-Tabara") rebels in several villages north of Minembwe, including Mikenge, Tuwetuwe, and Kalingi, as well as in the Kamombo and Bijombo *groupements*.[[38]](#cite_note-:4-38) Additional clashes were reported south of Minembwe around 2 February, notably in Muliza and Kakenge villages. These clashes formed part of an ongoing FARDC offensive aimed at retaking Minembwe, following the recapture of Point Zéro village near Minembwe on 29 January.[[38]](#cite_note-:4-38)[[39]](#cite_note-39)[[40]](#cite_note-40) Meanwhile, on 31 January and 1 February, [Kisangani Bangoka International Airport](/wiki/Kisangani_Bangoka_International_Airport "Kisangani Bangoka International Airport") came under attack by [kamikaze drones](/wiki/Kamikaze_drones "Kamikaze drones") carrying unconventional [payloads](/wiki/Payload "Payload") made up of several [submunitions](/wiki/Cluster_munition "Cluster munition"). The Tshopo provincial government blamed Rwanda and its M23 allies, denouncing the strike as a "barbaric, unjust, and ongoing aggression" against the DRC. FARDC forces and their security partners intercepted and neutralized eight drones before they reached their targets, and no casualties or significant damage were reported.[[41]](#cite_note-41)[[42]](#cite_note-42)[[43]](#cite_note-43)[[44]](#cite_note-44) The rebel movement claimed to have carried out the "destruction of the drone command center installed at Kisangani airport", from where the army conducts attacks in the areas under its occupation, "notably in Masisi, Walikale, Rutshuru, Lubero, Kalehe, Mwenga, Minembwe".[[45]](#cite_note-45) On 2 February, the Congolese government and AFC-M23 advanced the Doha peace process by signing the mandate for the [Ceasefire Monitoring and Verification Mechanism](/wiki/Ceasefire_Monitoring_and_Verification_Mechanism "Ceasefire Monitoring and Verification Mechanism") during a meeting in Doha that brought together regional and international actors who agreed to strengthen monitoring, verification, and information-sharing, including the deployment of an initial MONUSCO mission to Uvira to monitor the ceasefire on the ground.[[46]](#cite_note-46)[[47]](#cite_note-47) On 3 February, FARDC-Wazalendo forces repelled an M23 attack in Kalinga, Kisimba *groupement*, and forced them to withdraw toward Mpombi in Balinda, after which M23 bombarded Kalinga village despite it remaining under FARDC-Wazalendo control.[[48]](#cite_note-48) In Rutshuru Territory, M23 fighters advancing from Mweso in Masisi Territory launched simultaneous attacks on Wazalendo positions overlooking Kivuma and Kanyangohe in Bwito Chiefdom, but were ultimately pushed back toward Mweso.[[49]](#cite_note-49) On 8–9 February, FARDC carried out drone strikes on M23 positions in Mindjendje, reportedly wounding several rebels amid renewed clashes as the group reinforced its positions around Pinga.[[50]](#cite_note-50)

### 10–20 February

On 10 February, amid intensified international ceasefire efforts, M23 announced in Tshanzu, Rutshuru Territory, the completion of a training program integrating 7,532 new fighters into its armed wing, the *Armée révolutionnaire congolaise* (ARC), a figure that could not be independently verified.[[51]](#cite_note-51) The following day, 11 February, FARDC carried out further airstrikes against M23 positions near Mpeti,[[52]](#cite_note-52) while the M23-RDF coalition reportedly set fire to several civilian homes in Rusankuku, in the [Fizi](/wiki/Fizi_Territory "Fizi Territory")-[Uvira](/wiki/Uvira_Territory "Uvira Territory") highlands.[[53]](#cite_note-53) On 12 February, M23 withdrew from the strategic Buhaya hill overlooking Katobi, about 15 km from [Pinga](/wiki/Pinga,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Pinga, Democratic Republic of the Congo"), a position it had held since displacing Wazalendo in November 2025, for reasons that remained unclear.[[54]](#cite_note-54) That same day, the UN Special Representative and Interim Head of MONUSCO, Vivian van de Perre,[[55]](#cite_note-55) began a visit to [Goma](/wiki/Goma "Goma") to engage stakeholders and advance preparations for the [Ceasefire Monitoring and Verification Mechanism](/wiki/Ceasefire_Monitoring_and_Verification_Mechanism "Ceasefire Monitoring and Verification Mechanism"), landing by UN helicopter at [Goma International Airport](/wiki/Goma_International_Airport "Goma International Airport") for the first time since its closure following the rebel takeover of the city.[[56]](#cite_note-56) On 13 February, unidentified armed men raided Kateku village in the Ikobo *groupement* during the night, looting livestock, mobile phones, cash, and other valuables, with residents accusing M23, which controls the area, of being responsible.[[57]](#cite_note-57) On 15 February, nine M23 fighters surrendered to Wazalendo in Chanjikiro, handing over four [AK-47](/wiki/AK-47 "AK-47") rifles amid sustained FARDC pressure on rebel positions around Pinga.[[58]](#cite_note-58) On 17 and 18 February, joint FARDC-Wazalendo forces fought M23-affiliated militias in several villages surrounding [Minembwe](/wiki/Minembwe "Minembwe"), and on 18 February FARDC reported killing and capturing an unspecified number of newly trained M23 recruits who had graduated from the group's Tchanzu training facility. Additional fighting on 19 February involved Twirwaneho and Ngumino fighters aligned with M23 in the highlands near Minembwe, including in Tuwetuwe, Bilalombili, and Mikenge, as both sides contested control of Point Zéro village.[[59]](#cite_note-59) The FARDC General Staff added that RDF and M23 elements conducted coordinated assaults on 18–19 February against FARDC positions in Ihula (Walikale Territory) and on the hills of Kazaraho, Bugabo, Cahi, and Virumbi (Rutshuru Territory), North Kivu.[[60]](#cite_note-:5-60) In South Kivu, FARDC attributed additional attacks in Mikenge, Kalonge, Kahungwe, Kanga, and Lugeje to a coalition of RDF, M23, Twirwaneho, FNL, Nzabapema, and RED-Tabara, reporting four houses destroyed and the death of 18-year-old Matabishi Siston Azini.[[60]](#cite_note-:5-60)

### 21–28 February

On 22 February, M23 clashed with Wazalendo in Kasenyi, Chugi, and Kinigi in Bahunde Chiefdom of Masisi Territory near the mining town of [Rubaya](/wiki/Rubaya_mines "Rubaya mines"), with Wazalendo retaking the three towns, previously regarded as strategic M23 strongholds, and reportedly seizing military equipment during the fighting.[[61]](#cite_note-:6-61) M23 rebels reinforced their troops on "Sita" hill to curb the opposing advance and attempt to retake Chugi and Kinigi, while separately reporting that FARDC carried out drone strikes in several parts of Masisi Territory.[[61]](#cite_note-:6-61) On 23 February, Wazalendo continued their push toward Runigi, Kabara, Kiruli, and Kanyalu in the direction of Rubaya,[[62]](#cite_note-62) while that same day the Kavimvira crossing, a key trade route linking [Uvira](/wiki/Uvira "Uvira") and [Bujumbura](/wiki/Bujumbura "Bujumbura"), resumed operations after Congolese authorities regained control of the area.[[63]](#cite_note-63) On 24 February, FARDC drones struck M23 targets in Kishusha, near Rubaya, as clashes intensified again around Rubaya and elsewhere in Masisi Territory. [Willy Ngoma](/wiki/Willy_Ngoma "Willy Ngoma"), spokesperson for M23's armed wing, was reportedly killed in a strike in Rubaya.[[64]](#cite_note-64)[[65]](#cite_note-65)[[66]](#cite_note-66) Preliminary accounts suggested several senior M23 figures were injured, though [Sultani Makenga](/wiki/Sultani_Makenga "Sultani Makenga") had reportedly departed the area prior to the attack. In South Kivu, FARDC is reportedly benefiting from contractors allegedly linked to [Erik Prince](/wiki/Erik_Prince "Erik Prince"), who are said to be aiding FARDC operations against Twirwaneho forces in the highlands.[[67]](#cite_note-:7-67) Meanwhile, the [FDNB](/wiki/Burundi_National_Defence_Force "Burundi National Defence Force"), regarded as FARDC's principal regional ally, redeployed troops in large numbers via Lake Tanganyika beginning in late December and early January. By 25 February, approximately 5,000 FDNB forces were fighting alongside FARDC and Wazalendo forces in South Kivu.[[67]](#cite_note-:7-67) On 26 February, two civilians were killed during an exchange of fire between M23 fighters and an unidentified armed group in the JTN concession in [Bwito Chiefdom](/wiki/Bwito_Chiefdom "Bwito Chiefdom").[[68]](#cite_note-68) In Masisi Territory, Chugi, Katobo, and Kaniro, which are strategic villages controlling access to Rubaya, returned to M23 control after clashes with Wazalendo. Although Wazalendo had captured them on 23 February, they withdrew after four days following a reinforced M23 counteroffensive.[[69]](#cite_note-69) That same day, Kasenyi and Luke also fell back under M23 control.[[70]](#cite_note-70)

March 2026
----------

### 1–2 March

On 1 March, Bangboka International Airport was again targeted in a series of drone attacks that caused no casualties. One drone was intercepted around 3 p.m., followed by three additional "kamikaze" drones neutralized between 5 p.m. and 7 p.m. The governor of Tshopo blamed M23 and Rwanda for the attack.[[71]](#cite_note-:8-71) According to the Congolese research institute Ebuteli, FARDC has employed Turkish-made [TAI Anka](/wiki/TAI_Anka "TAI Anka") drones and Chinese [CH-4](/wiki/CASC_Rainbow#CH-4 "CASC Rainbow") combat drones. The same source alleges that the airport attack involved Turkish [Baykar](/wiki/Baykar "Baykar") [YIHA-III](/wiki/YIHA-III "YIHA-III") drones, while M23 reportedly operated Estonian-made Threod Systems drones.[[71]](#cite_note-:8-71) M23 later claimed responsibility, stating that the operation to "neutralize and destroy drones" that it alleges were preparing to be launched to "massacre civilians and attack its positions" near Kisangani.[[72]](#cite_note-72) That same 1 March, M23 recaptured the Katoyi sector in southern Masisi Territory and Buhimba village in Walikale Territory's Waloa-Yungu *groupement* after attacking FARDC positions from Kilongo and forcing them to retreat to Ngenge, a locality west of Buhimba.[[73]](#cite_note-73)[[74]](#cite_note-74) On 2 March, FARDC–Wazalendo forces repelled an M23 assault on the strategic Fungura Hill, which overlooks Katobi in the Kisimba *groupement* of Walikale Territory, after the rebels advanced from Mpeti. The hill is considered a key defensive position protecting [Pinga](/wiki/Pinga,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Pinga, Democratic Republic of the Congo"), and its capture could have created a corridor toward nearby settlements.[[75]](#cite_note-75) Meanwhile, in Rutshuru Territory, M23 released more than 5,000 FARDC soldiers in [Rumangabo](/wiki/Rumangabo "Rumangabo") and handed them over to the [International Committee of the Red Cross](/wiki/International_Committee_of_the_Red_Cross "International Committee of the Red Cross") (ICRC) for repatriation to Kinshasa.[[76]](#cite_note-76) In May 2025, the ICRC had already facilitated the transfer of more than 1,300 disarmed soldiers from Goma to Kinshasa.[[77]](#cite_note-:10-77) On the diplomatic front the same day, the [United States Department of the Treasury](/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury "United States Department of the Treasury") imposed sanctions on the RDF and four senior officials, such as Army Chief of Staff Vincent Nyakarundi, Major General Ruki Karusisi, Chief of Defence Staff Mubarakh Muganga, and Special Operations commander Stanislas Gashugi, for supporting, training, and fighting alongside M23 in eastern DRC.[[78]](#cite_note-78)

### 3–13 March

On 3 March, M23 fighters advancing mainly from the Ndete and Kasopo axes captured Kazinga village in the Nyamaboko I *groupement* of Masisi Territory, roughly two weeks after losing it to FARDC forces. Kazinga is a strategic locality in the Osso-Banyungu sector that provides access to Buhimba and the Katoyi sector.[[79]](#cite_note-79) On 5 March, M23 fighters withdrew without clashes from Buabo and Ndete villages in the same sector. Wazalendo elements reoccupied both villages on 6 March. The reasons for M23's withdrawal remain unclear, though some local observers suggest government forces may have exerted pressure while attempting to encircle this strategic zone.[[80]](#cite_note-80) Meanwhile, on the diplomatic front that same day, 6 March, the [State Department](/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State") announced visa restrictions on "several senior Rwandan officials" accused of fueling instability in eastern DRC. Washington stated that such actions violate the [Washington Agreements](/wiki/2025_DRC%E2%80%93Rwanda_peace_agreement "2025 DRC–Rwanda peace agreement") and contribute to violence in the [Great Lakes](/wiki/African_Great_Lakes "African Great Lakes") region.[[81]](#cite_note-81) On 7 March, an airstrike attributed to FARDC targeted an M23 position in the Mushaki area of Masisi Territory. The strike reportedly hit a rebel position located on the farm of businessman and former national deputy [Édouard Mwangachuchu](/wiki/%C3%89douard_Mwangachuchu "Édouard Mwangachuchu") and was carried out using a military drone.[[82]](#cite_note-:9-82) M23 political spokesperson Lawrence Kanyuka said the attack caused civilian casualties, although several local sources reported that no civilian losses had been immediately confirmed in surrounding villages.[[82]](#cite_note-:9-82) On 9 March, FARDC–Wazalendo forces dislodged M23 rebels from their position in Mukole in the Kisimba *groupement*. The Mukole capture provides FARDC a strategic vantage point from which they can monitor nearby localities, including Ihula and Kalonge in Walikale Territory and Kalembe in neighboring Masisi Territory, areas still held by M23.[[83]](#cite_note-83) In separate clashes, FARDC–Wazalendo forces also recaptured Kazinga village in Masisi Territory.[[84]](#cite_note-:11-84) On 10 March, FARDC reported shooting down several [one-way attack drones](/wiki/One-way_attack_drone "One-way attack drone") over Mikenge village in Mwenga Territory, claiming that the drones were operated by RDF and M23-affiliated fighters. Pro-government media also alleged that the RDF deployed a helicopter to [Minembwe](/wiki/Minembwe "Minembwe").[[84]](#cite_note-:11-84) That same day, 18 former M23 rebels who had surrendered the previous week in Chanjikiro (Kisimba *groupement*, Walikale Territory) arrived in Pinga. The defectors cited sustained military pressure from FARDC–Wazalendo forces, as well as harsh living conditions within the rebel movement, as reasons for their surrender.[[85]](#cite_note-85)[[77]](#cite_note-:10-77) On 11 March, drone strikes reportedly hit the Himbi *quartier* in the Goma commune, which damaged homes, injuring several civilians, and killing three people, including Karine Buisset, a UNICEF specialist working on protection against sexual exploitation and abuse. The identities of the other two victims have not been officially confirmed.[[86]](#cite_note-86)[[77]](#cite_note-:10-77) On 12 March, in [Kinshasa](/wiki/Kinshasa "Kinshasa"), the Deputy Prime Minister of Defense held talks with François Moreillon of the ICRC regarding the release of [prisoners of war](/wiki/Prisoner_of_war "Prisoner of war") in eastern DRC. Moreillon stated that the discussions were part of the Doha peace process and aligned with the ICRC's mandate as a neutral intermediary.[[77]](#cite_note-:10-77)

### 14–20 March

On 14 March, Wazalendo seized Malemo village in the [Bashali-Mokoto *groupement*](/wiki/Bashali_Chiefdom#Groupements_and_localités "Bashali Chiefdom") of [Bashali Chiefdom](/wiki/Bashali_Chiefdom "Bashali Chiefdom") after engaging M23 rebels. The village lies along the Kalembe–Mpeti axis, which is a strategic route that connects Masisi Territory to Walikale Territory. After the clashes, some M23 fighters withdrew toward Kalembe village in the eastern part of the *groupement*, while others retreated to Mindjendje and Mpeti in the Kisimba *groupement* of Walikale Territory.[[87]](#cite_note-87) On 15 March, FARDC–Wazalendo forces carried out clashes and drone strikes against Twirwaneho positions near Minembwe, particularly around Kalingi. On 16 March, Wazalendo fighters from the Virunga National Park area reportedly attacked M23 positions in Mugunga and Lac Vert *quartiers* in the western Goma, but M23 forces repelled the assault, and reportedly killed at least 13 Wazalendo fighters.[[88]](#cite_note-:12-88) That same day, FARDC arrested two Wazalendo leaders active in Kalehe Territory, Masisi Territory, and Walikale Territory, citing human rights violations and ongoing internal conflicts between them.[[88]](#cite_note-:12-88) On 17 March, Malemo returned to M23 control after Wazalendo forces voluntarily withdrew to their previous positions, which allowed M23 to reoccupy the village without resistance. The reasons for the withdrawal remain unclear, though reports suggest that M23 elements regrouped in Mpeti and Kalembe took advantage of the situation to return.[[89]](#cite_note-89)

In South Kivu, multiple clashes were reported between M23 and FARDC–Wazalendo forces. On 15 March, Wazalendo fighters reportedly blocked civilian movement along the RN5 at Kasambura, about three miles south of Kabunambo, accusing certain residents of spying for M23. A local source told [Actualite.cd](/wiki/Actualite.cd "Actualite.cd") on 18 March that the closure may have been requested by the Burundian government, which suspected M23 infiltration from the Ruzizi Plain.[[90]](#cite_note-90) On 17 March, heavy fighting was reported around [Runingu](/wiki/Runingu "Runingu"), Biriba, Kabunambo, and [Sange](/wiki/Sange,_Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Sange, Democratic Republic of the Congo") along the RN5, as well as in midland areas west of the road. Senior M23 officials and affiliated sources claimed that DRC coalition forces initiated the clashes and shelled their positions. However, FARDC denied these accusations on 18 March, also rejecting claims that M23 had captured Kirungu or made advances in the Ruzizi Plain.[[91]](#cite_note-91)[[92]](#cite_note-:02-92) Pro-government journalists reported that FARDC forces prevented M23 from advancing toward Kirungu after the rebels seized Mitamba. Journalist Daniel Michombero dismissed claims that Congolese authorities had fled to Bujumbura or Baraka and called them false. He also alleged that Rwandan troops had crossed into the DRC via the Ruzizi II border post. Meanwhile, M23 sources accused FARDC and its allies of carrying out drone strikes on Twirwaneho positions in Bidegu and Mikenge near Minembwe on 17 March. FARDC later stated on 19 March that 20 M23 fighters had surrendered in Mikenge.[[92]](#cite_note-:02-92)

On the diplomatic front, senior military and political officials, along with envoys representing President [Félix Tshisekedi](/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Tshisekedi "Félix Tshisekedi") and President [Paul Kagame](/wiki/Paul_Kagame "Paul Kagame"), took part in bilateral talks with U.S. officials on 17 March, followed by a trilateral meeting in Washington, D.C., on 18 March. After the discussions, the U.S., the DRC, and Rwanda issued a joint statement announcing that both African countries had agreed to undertake "a series of coordinated steps" to reduce tensions and improve conditions on the ground.[[92]](#cite_note-:02-92) The U.S. urged both sides to fully implement the military plan outlined in the U.S.-brokered framework by the end of 2026, with an initial evaluation scheduled before 15 April. Washington also called on the DRC to neutralize the FDLR and on Rwanda to withdraw its troops and equipment. Six operational zones were identified for anti-FDLR operations, five located in M23-controlled areas and one in a government-controlled zone.[[92]](#cite_note-:02-92) During the 18 March trilateral meeting, [Massad Boulos](/wiki/Massad_Boulos "Massad Boulos") warned that the U.S. could impose sanctions on either party if they failed to honor their commitments. Meanwhile, on 17 March, Bruno Lemarquis, the UN humanitarian coordinator and acting head of the UN mission in the DRC, met with [Corneille Nangaa](/wiki/Corneille_Nangaa "Corneille Nangaa") in Goma to address the humanitarian crisis in eastern DRC, following his condemnation of 11 March drone strikes on the city.[[92]](#cite_note-:02-92)

### 21– 31 March

On 22 March, Wazalendo attacked M23 in Kanune village, located roughly 19 miles west of [Kanyabayonga](/wiki/Kanyabayonga "Kanyabayonga") in Rutshuru Territory, within the Ikobo *groupement* of eastern Walikale Territory, before withdrawing afterward. The next day, March 23, FARDC–Wazalendo forces repelled an M23 assault on their positions in Kazinga village.[[93]](#cite_note-93)[[94]](#cite_note-94)[[95]](#cite_note-95) In Walikale Territory, Wazalendo also blocked an M23 maneuver aimed at outflanking FARDC-Wazalendo positions along the RP529 and advancing toward Mungazi village, which is situated about seven miles west of M23's frontline in Kibati and 21 miles west of Nyabiondo in Masisi Territory.[[96]](#cite_note-:13-96) Between 23–24 March, M23 unilaterally pulled back troops and equipment from over a dozen frontline positions in northern [Lubero Territory](/wiki/Lubero_Territory "Lubero Territory"), including Kipese, around eight miles southeast of [Lubero town](/wiki/Lubero "Lubero"), although it retained its forward base in Katondi on the RN2, about 10 miles south of Lubero. Despite the withdrawal, some M23 forces continued to move further south.[[96]](#cite_note-:13-96) On 26 March, M23 withdrew from multiple locations in Walikale Territory, including Minjenje, Mpeti, and Kanune.[[97]](#cite_note-97) Clashes resumed on 27 March, when M23 fought Wazalendo and regained control of two villages in the Katoyi sector, including Mitimingi in southern Masisi Territory, roughly 26 miles southwest of Rubaya. On 28 March, M23 withdrew from Pitakongo and Bunyatenge in Lubero Territory; Bunyatenge is a remote gold-mining village about 17 miles west of Bingi. After these withdrawals, officials appointed by M23 reportedly fled, and Wazalendo fighters reoccupied several villages. In Walikale Territory, M23 repelled a Wazalendo attack in Ihula village.[[98]](#cite_note-:14-98)

On 29 March, M23 attacked again and recaptured three villages in the Nyamaboko *groupement*, including Mukohwa, while FARDC and Wazalendo forces repelled separate M23 assaults in Ngululu and Kazinga. Wazalendo also regained control of Mpeti along RP1030 after M23's withdrawal.[[98]](#cite_note-:14-98) That day, FARDC Deputy Chief of Staff General Jacques Ychaligonza announced deployment to Kisangani to begin operations against the FDLR to disarm them voluntarily or by force. He stated that three battalions had been mobilized to track and neutralize FDLR fighters, who would be detained in Kisangani before being repatriated to Rwanda.[[98]](#cite_note-:14-98) Diplomatically, the Doha peace process is expected to move to [Switzerland](/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") in April 2026, where talks between the Congolese government and M23 are set to resume. On 25 March, Foreign Minister [Thérèse Kayikwamba Wagner](/wiki/Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_Kayikwamba_Wagner "Thérèse Kayikwamba Wagner") noted that the process had stalled, partly due to Qatar's limitations in hosting talks amid the [Middle East conflicts](/wiki/2026_Iran_war "2026 Iran war").[[98]](#cite_note-:14-98)

April 2026
----------

### 1 April

On 1 April, Wazalendo forces repelled M23 attacks in several villages west of Uvira, including Mitamba, about 15 miles away. In Masisi Territory, clashes also appeared in Kashovu village, approximately 26 miles southwest of Rubaya.[[99]](#cite_note-99)
