## It Will Take Months to Get Oil and Gas Flowing out of the Persian Gulf

Some wells can be turned on in days or weeks, but bringing the Gulf’s energy system back to something akin to normal will take months.

Reopening the Strait of Hormuz — a central aim for the United States when it agreed to a cease-fire with Iran — would be the first step toward getting more energy flowing through the Persian Gulf.

But only the first step.

That is because dozens of refineries, storage facilities, and oil and gas fields in at least nine countries, from Iran to the United Arab Emirates and beyond, have been [targeted in strikes](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/03/20/business/energy-environment/iran-war-oil-gas-attacks.html). All told, 10 percent or more of the world’s oil supply has been turned off. Restarting those operations will require not only safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz, but also inspecting pumps, replacing bespoke processing equipment and recalling employees and ships that have scattered across the globe.

“It’s not a case of you just flick a switch and everything’s back up again,” said Martin Houston, a longtime oil and gas executive who now serves as board member for several energy companies.

The timeline for bringing the Gulf energy system back to some semblance of normal is highly uncertain. For one thing, the war has been formally paused for only two weeks. And the cease-fire appeared to be on shaky ground with attacks continuing across the region on Wednesday.

Under the deal that President Trump announced Tuesday evening, Iran [agreed](https://www.nytimes.com/live/2026/04/07/world/iran-war-trump-news/2272c0f5-7a7c-505a-94ba-0cf040bd556d?smid=url-share) to allow ships to pass through the strait without being attacked but there was [no sign on Wednesday that vessels were returning to the waterway](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/08/business/strait-hormuz-ships-traffic-iran-ceasefire.html) in large numbers. Earlier that day, Mr. Trump said that if the waterway remained closed, “a whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again.” He has also repeatedly threatened to strike Iranian power plants and other critical infrastructure if Iran does not allow vessels to pass through the strait — acts that could be [considered war crimes](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/24/us/politics/trump-iran-power-stations.html).

Attacks on energy facilities continued in the days leading up to the cease-fire, including on an [oil refinery in Kuwait](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/03/world/middleeast/infrastructure-attacked-gulf-kuwait-iran.html) and [petrochemical complexes in Iran](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/04/04/world/middleeast/israel-strikes-mahshahr-iran-oil.html). How much damage has already been done to the region’s infrastructure is difficult to know because many countries have shared little information.
