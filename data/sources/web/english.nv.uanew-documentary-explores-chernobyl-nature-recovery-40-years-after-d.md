# New documentary explores Chernobyl’s nature recovery 40 years after disaster

April 7, 2026, 06:00 PM

![Image 6: Documentary Bastion of the Exclusion Zone to air for the 40th anniversary of the Chernobyl disaster (Photo: Суспільне)](https://static.nv.ua/shared/system/Article/posters/003/270/596/original/c247dfd0ea501d1896f78cddf25f3381.png?q=85&stamp=20260407171530&w=900&f=webp)

Documentary Bastion of the Exclusion Zone to air for the 40th anniversary of the Chernobyl disaster (Photo: Суспільне)

[Ukraine’s public broadcaster Suspilne](https://corp.suspilne.media/novyny/64274-ekologichnyj-poglyad-na-katastrofu-u-filmi-suspilnogo-do-40-richchya-tragediyi-na-chaes/) has produced a documentary film,Bastion of the Exclusion Zone, marking the 40th anniversary of the Chernobyl disaster. The film explores dramatic changes in nature four decades after the catastrophe and is set to air and stream in April.

The anniversary falls on April 26, 2026, marking 40 years since the worst technological disaster in Europe — the accident at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant.

The film examines how nature has evolved in the exclusion zone without human interference over four decades, directors Olesia Morgunets-Isaienko and Roman Synchuk said.

![Image 7: Suspilne](https://static.nv.ua/shared/system/MediaPhoto/images/000/582/891/original/87be72aaccb2d6a9ad716085fcbd8caf.png?q=85&stamp=20260407171530&f=webp)

 Photo: Suspilne 

“Our film for the 40th anniversary of the Chernobyl disaster looks at the situation through an ecological lens,” Morgunets-Isaienko said. “It is a story about how humans and nature responded to the tragedy, how nature helped contain the spread of radiation, and how people are now trying to restore the disrupted water balance.”

![Image 8: Suspilne](https://static.nv.ua/shared/system/MediaPhoto/images/000/582/892/original/a250725e5032e0784e2dcb14e40516e7.png?q=85&stamp=20260407171531&f=webp)

 Photo: Suspilne 

The filmmakers argue the exclusion zone has become a natural barrier that helps contain radiation within its boundaries, with wetlands playing a key role in that process.

“Chernobyl has long been portrayed solely as a site of tragedy. But it is also a place of our mistakes — mistakes we continue to repeat,” said producer Volodymyr Ladyzhenets. “With this film, we wanted to shift the perspective and show that nature can recover without human intervention, while human interference is not always the solution. Perhaps the main lesson of Chernobyl is that sometimes the best decision is not to interfere.”

The documentary combines newly filmed footage from the exclusion zone with archival material from the Central State Audiovisual and Electronic Archive of Ukraine, including previously unreleased footage from Suspilne’s media archive and personal collections of researchers working in the Chernobyl Radiation and Ecological Biosphere Reserve and the 10-kilometer zone.

The film also features commentary from ecologists, emergency service workers, and researchers, alongside footage of wildlife that now inhabits the zone, including deer, elk, Przewalski’s horses, fish, and birds.
