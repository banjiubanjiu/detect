After threatening Iran with the destruction of an entire “civilization,” Donald Trump has heightened global tensions and underscored what critics describe as an unprecedented level of unpredictability in U.S. policy on Apr. 8.

A number of leading U.S. media outlets wrote about it, noting that they routinely analyze and fact-check dozens of Trump’s statements but were nevertheless struck this time by the tone of his rhetoric toward Iran. The U.S. president’s April 7 morning post warning that “an entire civilization will die tonight — and it will never be restored” ultimately ended with the announcement of a two-week ceasefire with Iran, but stunned the United States, the Middle East and the world.

CNN said that in some ways “Trump was in his element”: “He was the central actor in a storm of his own making, spinning the planet around his own axis.” “Only the president knows the state of play and what he will do,” White House press secretary Karoline Leavitt said, as the world counted down the hours until Trump’s ultimatum to Iran expired (8:00 p.m. ET on April 7, 3:00 a.m. Kyiv time on April 8).

At the same time, CNN said Trump’s “chilling threat … crossed a line no American president had previously dared, or even wanted, to approach,” to the point that it was initially difficult to believe. His Truth Social post “most sharply yet raised questions about the temperament and judgment of the 79-year-old Trump.”

“Presidential words matter. Even publicly musing about mass killing of civilians is dangerous and inappropriate. The threat raised a silent question among those around Trump and across the country: is such behavior acceptable for the commander in chief of a global superpower armed with the deadliest weapons?” CNN wrote.

Despite ultimately stepping back from maximum escalation, the U.S. president “crossed moral and behavioral thresholds his predecessors in modern times never reached.” His remarks underscored that the United States — “long seen as a pillar of stability — is now, in the person of its president, the most unpredictable force in the world,” CNN concluded.

CNN also noted that for Trump’s critics, the episode became another повод to mock his “TACO” approach (Trump Always Chickens Out). Despite the intensity of his threats, it is another case in which the U.S. president took a maximalist position and then backed down, erasing his own red lines. “If Iran does gain control over access to the Strait of Hormuz during the two-week ceasefire, it will reinforce the perception that Trump has no good options in a war that has slipped beyond his control and that he is desperate to end,” the report said.

The Wall Street Journal called Trump’s April 7 post “the most dramatic ultimatum of his presidency.” “Even for a president who has long relied on maximalist threats, the 85-word post was stunning. It ricocheted from the Oval Office to foreign embassies and corporate offices, launching a countdown to Trump’s 8:00 p.m. ET deadline and a frantic global guessing game about what the world’s most powerful man was prepared to do.”

Amid Trump’s threats to bomb bridges, power plants and other civilian infrastructure in Iran — a country of 93 million people — “everyone from Pope Leo to actor Ben Stiller” urged him to stop, the Journal wrote.

The publication detailed the tense 24 hours as the world counted down to the April 7 deadline. “Administration officials fielded calls from executives and political allies trying to determine whether Trump’s threat was a bluff or a prelude to escalation. European officials huddled on a call and largely concluded Trump would back down. On Wall Street, traders and executives treated the day much like other Trump deadlines that came and went — as a negotiating tactic unlikely to end in catastrophe,” the article said.

The overall mood was captured by Peter Boockvar, chief investment officer at OnePoint BFG Wealth Partners: “It’s dizzying. One eye is on market screens and the other on Trump’s Truth Social page.”

At the same time, heated debates erupted among former allies and influencers in Trump’s orbit, including in livestreams and podcasts. “He’s trying to sound threatening and crazy,” said Tim Pool, a right-wing U.S. podcaster with more than 2 million subscribers, in comments to The Wall Street Journal. However, he added before the ceasefire was announced that if Trump failed to follow through, “we’ll see the emperor has no clothes. This will be his last gambit.”

The New York Times wrote that Trump’s latest “stunning” threats went beyond his usual bluffing and empty intimidation. “Trump threatened destruction that would be considered a war crime under international law,” the newspaper said.

The Times added that, in the view of the president and his supporters, the post threatening mass destruction of an entire country “is part of Trump’s chaotic negotiating style, aimed at creating a path to end a conflict of his own making and persuading Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz.” However, even given Trump’s tendency to make statements that go far beyond acceptable limits, these threats stand out, portraying him as “an impulsive leader accustomed to getting his way through coercion and unpredictability, but now unable to achieve his aims,” the author wrote.

Alex Wellerstein, a historian who studies nuclear conflict, said that even without these threats being carried out, Trump’s violent rhetoric undermines trust in him as a negotiator and in the U.S. position globally.

“We are talking about a world that increasingly sees the United States as erratic and dangerous rather than a reliable partner,” he said. “Countries that usually align themselves with democracy and freedom are now on the other side from the United States.”

On April 7, Trump said he had agreed to a two-week ceasefire with Iran on the condition that the Strait of Hormuz be reopened immediately. He said the United States had already “achieved and exceeded all military objectives” and had “made significant progress” toward a final peace agreement.

At the same time, Iran’s Supreme National Security Council also declared “victory” following the ceasefire, saying the “enemy had been driven into a state of historic helplessness and ultimate defeat.” In a statement carried by Iranian state media, Tehran’s proposed 10-point peace plan includes regulating passage through the Strait of Hormuz, ending attacks on Iran and its regional proxies, withdrawing U.S. troops from the region, compensating Iran, lifting international sanctions and unfreezing assets, as well as a binding U.N. resolution to secure a final peace agreement.
