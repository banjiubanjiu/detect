As the civil war enters [its third year](https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/horn-africa/sudan/battle-khartoum-marks-crossroads-sudans-civil-war), Sudan’s two warring factions remain [locked](https://acleddata.com/conflict-watchlist-2025/sudan/) in a deadly power struggle. Death toll estimates vary widely, with the former U.S. envoy for Sudan suggesting as many as [four hundred thousand](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/08/30/opinion/sudan-genocide-famine.html) have been killed since the conflict began on April 15, 2023. More than [eleven million](https://data.unhcr.org/en/documents/details/119411) have been displaced, giving rise to the [worst displacement crisis](https://www.doctorswithoutborders.org/latest/photos-worlds-largest-displacement-crisis) in the world. Over [four million displaced Sudanese](https://data.unhcr.org/en/documents/details/119411) have fled to unstable areas in [Chad](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/conflict/violent-extremism-sahel), [Ethiopia](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/conflict/conflict-ethiopia), and [South Sudan](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/conflict/civil-war-south-sudan), [overwhelming](https://www.unrefugees.org/news/sudan-crisis-explained/) refugee camps. The UN continues to [plead](https://news.un.org/en/story/2024/12/1158396#:~:text=Staggering%20scale%20of%20Sudan%20crisis%20'demands%20sustained%20and%20urgent%20attention',-By%20Vibhu%20Mishra&text=A%20senior%20UN%20humanitarian%20official,need%20of%20aid%20and%20protection.) for more support as more than [thirty million](https://www.rescue.org/article/crisis-sudan-what-happening-and-how-help) need humanitarian assistance, and deteriorating food security risks are triggering the “[world’s largest hunger crisis](https://reliefweb.int/report/sudan/catastrophic-year-sudan#:~:text=Over%2025%20million%20people%20across,world's%20largest%20hunger%20crisis%20\(WFP\)).”  

Meanwhile, mediation efforts [have failed](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/uae-proposes-sudan-ceasefire-army-rejects-call-2025-02-11/) to produce results as the leaders of the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and Rapid Support Forces (RSF) refuse to halt their violence, and regional and international actors have [taken sides](https://www.wilsoncenter.org/article/conflict-sudan-map-regional-and-international-actors) in the war. As conditions deteriorate, the World Food Programme’s Carl Skau [warned](https://press.un.org/en/2024/sc15634.doc.htm), “we are running out of time.”  

## Background  
For the first half of the twentieth century, Sudan was a [joint protectorate](https://uca.edu/politicalscience/home/research-projects/dadm-project/middle-eastnorth-africapersian-gulf-region/anglo-egyptian-sudan-1922-1956/#:~:text=Sudan%20formally%20attained%20its%20independence,were%20killed%20during%20the%20crisis.) of Egypt and the United Kingdom, known as the Anglo-Egyptian Condominium. Egypt and the United Kingdom signed a treaty [relinquishing](https://uca.edu/politicalscience/home/research-projects/dadm-project/sub-saharan-africa-region/70-republic-of-sudan-1956-present/) sovereignty to the independent Republic of Sudan in 1956. The stark[ internal divide](https://www.jstor.org/stable/45313944) between the country’s wealthier northern region, which was majority Arab and Muslim, and its less-developed southern region, which was majority Christian or animist, sparked two civil wars, the second of which would see the country [split](https://carnegieendowment.org/2012/05/16/sudan-from-conflict-to-conflict-pub-48140) into two states in 2011. The second Sudanese [civil war](https://peaceaccords.nd.edu/accord/sudan-comprehensive-peace-agreement) from 1983 to 2005 [killed](https://www.pbs.org/frontlineworld/stories/sudan/facts.html) an estimated two million people, with widespread documentation of famine and [atrocities](https://sites.tufts.edu/atrocityendings/2015/08/07/sudan-2nd-civil-war-darfur/). In July 2011, Sudan’s southern territory seceded and formed a new state: [the Republic of South Sudan](https://www.cfr.org/global-conflict-tracker/conflict/civil-war-south-sudan).  

The[ dictatorship](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-16010445) of Omar al-Bashir defined Sudan’s post-colonial period. Bashir seized power in a [1989 coup](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/sudans-bashir-admits-role-1989-coup-during-trial-2022-12-20/) after serving in the Egyptian military during condominium rule and later as an officer in the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF). As president, he oversaw the Second Sudanese Civil War, the secession of South Sudan, and the conflict in Darfur. The [Darfur war](https://reliefweb.int/report/sudan/understanding-darfur-conflict), which broke out in 2003, was later condemned by the International Criminal Court (ICC) as a[ genocide](https://www.icc-cpi.int/darfur) targeting non-Arab populations, including the Fur, Zaghawa, and Masalit peoples in western Sudan. During his [regime](https://apnews.com/article/ap-top-news-osama-bin-laden-international-news-omar-al-bashir-cairo-8c637e57658243aca3ee36318a6b5e20), Bashir enforced a strict interpretation of[ sharia](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-12033185), employed private militias and [morality police](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/11/29/world/africa/sudan-moral-policing-law-al-bashir.html) to enforce his decrees, and [persecuted](https://www.state.gov/reports/2019-report-on-international-religious-freedom/sudan/) Christianity, Sunni apostasy, Shiism, and other minority religious activities. Bashir’s regime survived until 2019. By the final decade of his presidency, Bashir faced increasing popular[ ](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-46761119)protests [calling](https://www.cfr.org/articles/president-bashir-facing-pressure-protests-sudan) for democracy, access to essential services, and a new system of governance. The revolution culminated in an April 2019[ coup](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-47852496), which was carried out [jointly](https://www.cnn.com/2019/04/11/africa/sudan-unrest-intl/index.html) by the SAF—led by General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan—and the Rapid Security Forces (RSF), a militia led by Mohamed Hamdan “Hemedti” Dagalo.  

The RSF is the most powerful paramilitary group to emerge from the Bashir era. The RSF [evolved](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/4/16/sudan-unrest-what-is-the-rapid-support-forces#:~:text=Who%20are%20Sudan's%20RSF%3F&text=Sudan's%20military%20and%20the%20Rapid,fears%20of%20a%20civil%20war.) from the [Janjaweed militia](https://unterm.un.org/unterm2/en/view/d5eb4654-b0ed-4f5c-894d-92a33e2d574d), an Arab-majority armed group funded by Bashir to suppress southern Sudanese rebels and [fight](https://www.aljazeera.com/gallery/2023/2/26/photos-the-lasting-scars-and-pain-of-the-war-in-darfur-sudan) in the Darfur War. The group committed [brutal attacks](https://www.hrw.org/report/2015/09/09/men-no-mercy/rapid-support-forces-attacks-against-civilians-darfur-sudan) and crimes across the Darfur region, including mass displacement, sexual violence, and kidnapping. The first two years of the conflict in Darfur claimed over [two hundred thousand lives](https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/darfur), with over one hundred thousand more since 2005.  

With Bashir’s support, the loosely coordinated Janjaweed was formally [organized](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/04/17/world/africa/paramilitary-rsf-explainer.html) under the RSF banner in 2013. Since then, the RSF has been employed as a border guard force, a source of mercenaries for the Saudi coalition in the Yemeni war, and a hired security force to [repress](https://acleddata.com/2019/07/02/the-rapid-support-forces-and-the-escalation-of-violence-in-sudan/) popular uprisings. RSF leader Hemedti became one of Sudan’s [wealthiest](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-sudan-gold-exclusive/exclusive-sudan-militia-leader-grew-rich-by-selling-gold-idUSKBN1Y01DQ) men by seizing control of gold mines.  

Before 2019, Bashir hired the RSF to protect him from coups and assassination attempts. Despite this, the RSF ultimately joined forces with the SAF in the 2019 coup to oust Bashir and establish a [transitional government](https://www.usip.org/publications/2020/05/sudan-one-year-after-bashir) and a new constitution. Burhan led the [Transitional Sovereignty Council](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2021/11/11/sudan-army-chief-issues-a-decree-for-new-sovereign-council) with Hemedti as his deputy, alongside other military leaders and several civilians.  

Among the civilian members, the council[ chose](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-49425702) Abdalla Hamdok, an economist and [development expert](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2022/1/3/profile-abdalla-hamdok-sudans-outgoing-civilian-leader), as prime minister. During his brief tenure, he attempted to [mitigate](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2020/12/15/unacceptable-sudanese-pm-criticises-armys-business-interests) Sudan’s extreme economic turmoil and project stability to the outside world. However, the SAF and RSF orchestrated a coup against Hamdok in October 2021 and suspended the constitution. In response, international institutions such as the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund [paused](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/05/03/us/politics/us-sudan-democracy-war.html#:~:text=After%20the%20military%20coup%20in,forgive%20%2450%20billion%20of%20debt.) badly needed debt relief and other aid to Sudan. [Mass demonstrations](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2022/10/26/one-killed-on-sudan-anti-coup-protest-anniversary) demanding a return to civilian control intensified in Khartoum.  

Hamdok was [briefly reinstated](https://www.nytimes.com/2022/01/02/world/africa/abdalla-hamdok-resigns-sudan.html) as prime minister in November 2021 after [agreeing](https://www.cfr.org/in-brief/crisis-sudan-what-know) to concede certain governing powers to Burhan and Hemedti. However, he ultimately[ resigned](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-59855246) in January 2022, as Sudanese [protestors](https://www.africanews.com/2022/02/24/un-urges-end-to-sudan-crackdown/?utm_term=Autofeed&utm_medium=AfricanewsEN&utm_source=Twitter#Echobox=1645739864) were [dissatisfied](https://www.cfr.org/articles/sudanese-protesters-insist-end-military-rule) with the terms of his reinstatement and the violent actions of security forces, who had repeatedly[ beaten](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-africa-59795213) and killed protestors. Since Hamdok’s resignation, Sudan has had no effective civilian leadership, with Burhan operating as [de facto](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/4/16/who-is-al-burhan-sudans-military-de-facto-head-of-state#:~:text=As%20head%20of%20the%20SC,short%2Dlived%20path%20to%20democracy.) _ _ head of state. By early 2022, Burhan and Hemedti were left at the helm of the government, with the power to direct its [democratic transition](https://www.cfr.org/articles/credibility-short-supply-sudan).  

Negotiations throughout 2022 over the future of Sudanese governance culminated in a [December 2022 deal](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/sudanese-civilian-parties-sign-framework-deal-new-political-transition-2022-12-05/) laying the groundwork for a two-year transition to civilian leadership and national elections. Many citizens [rejected the plan](https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/horn-africa/sudan/critical-window-bolster-sudans-next-government) due to the contested time frame, the security sector retaining some post-transition state powers, and the failure to hold Burhan, Hemedti, and other security sector figures accountable for violence. Unrest broke out again and persisted from December into the [spring](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/3/1/sudanese-protester-killed-in-weekly-demonstrations), leading to more [violent crackdowns on protestors](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/protests-continue-sudans-capital-days-after-political-deal-2022-12-08/).  

Major sticking points emerged as the transitional government began to negotiate a plan. Foremost was the role of Hemedti and the RSF; the agreement [elevated](https://www.theafricareport.com/300976/explainer-sudan-generals-deadly-fight-for-power/) Hemedti to Burhan’s equal, promoting him from general’s deputy. The deal also called for the eventual [integration](https://acleddata.com/2023/04/14/sudan-situation-update-april-2023-political-process-to-form-a-transitional-civilian-government-and-the-shift-in-disorder-trends/) of the RSF into Sudan’s legitimate armed forces under civilian leadership. However, the deal did not specify a [deadline](https://sudantribune.com/article272510/) for the RSF’s integration into the SAF due to disagreement between Burhan and Hemedti. The two leaders [missed](https://www.africanews.com/2023/04/12/sudan-fails-to-meet-deadline-to-form-civilian-govt/) an early 2023 deadline to determine conditions for the agreement’s implementation, indicating tension over the RSF-SAF relationship and the future of both forces as subordinates of an elected government.  

As the months passed, the power struggle between Burhan’s SAF and Hemedti’s RSF continued to stall the country’s transition efforts. [By early April](https://www.usip.org/publications/2023/04/whats-behind-fighting-sudan#:~:text=At%20its%20foundation%2C%20the%20fighting,of%20power%20in%20the%20state.), SAF troops lined the streets of Khartoum, and RSF soldiers were deployed throughout Sudan. [On April 15](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/5/31/fighting-in-sudan-a-timeline-of-key-events), a series of explosions shook Khartoum, along with heavy gunfire. SAF and RSF leadership both accused each other of firing first. The involvement of the [Wagner Group](https://www.cnn.com/2023/04/20/africa/wagner-sudan-russia-libya-intl/index.html) and foreign military influence, notably from the [United Arab Emirates](https://www.wsj.com/articles/a-u-s-ally-promised-to-send-aid-to-sudan-it-sent-weapons-instead-82d396f) (UAE),  has deepened the rivalry at the core of Sudan’s crisis.  

The [fighting in Khartoum ](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/least-40-killed-airstrike-southern-khartoum-market-local-volunteers-2023-09-10/)has persisted, and incidents of violence across the country continued to rise, including in Darfur. The [assassination](https://unitams.unmissions.org/en/unitams-statement-killing-governor-west-darfur) of West Darfur’s governor Khamis Abakar in June 2023, likely by [RSF militants](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2023/jun/15/sudan-paramilitary-forces-blamed-assassination-khamis-abdallah-abbakar-government-official), marked an escalation; [Abakar](https://www.cfr.org/articles/seeking-urgency-sudan) had accused the RSF of renewed genocidal attacks against minorities in Darfur. In June 2024, an [NGO report](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/world/sudan-war-leaves-towns-villages-burned-down-rcna156920) stated that over 235 fires had been set in villages across Sudan, with a majority set by militias in Darfur, since fighting erupted in mid-April 2023.  

Several NGOs, including [Human Rights Watch](https://www.hrw.org/world-report/2025/country-chapters/sudan), have documented evidence of numerous mass atrocities committed throughout the conflict, prompting [accusations](https://www.state.gov/reports/2023-country-reports-on-human-rights-practices/sudan) of ethnic cleansing and war crimes. In early November, RSF forces and allied militias killed [more than 800 people](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/un-says-more-than-800-people-reportedly-killed-in-attack-on-darfur-town-amid-war-in-sudan) in a multi-day rampage in Ardamata, a town in western Darfur. This attack reflected a [new surge of ethnically driven killings](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/darfur-refugees-report-new-spate-ethnically-driven-killings-2023-11-07/) targeting the Masalit in West Darfur. The UN High Commissioner for Refugees (UNCHR) Filippo Grandi [warned](https://www.politico.com/news/2023/11/11/un-sounds-alarm-on-darfur-warns-world-not-to-repeat-history-00126708#:~:text=At%20least%20800%20people%20were,its%20allies%20in%20West%20Darfur.&text=Escalating%20bloodshed%20since%20the%20outbreak,uptick%20of%20violence%20in%20Darfur.) that current violence is emblematic of the U.S.-recognized genocide in Darfur that killed an estimated 300,000 people between 2003 and 2005. A [statement](https://news.un.org/en/story/2024/04/1148536) made by the UN in January indicated that between ten thousand and fifteen thousand people were killed in 2023 due to ethnic violence by the RSF and its allies in West Darfur. In April 2024, U.S. Ambassador to the United Nations Linda Thomas-Greenfield [highlighted](https://usun.usmission.gov/remarks-by-ambassador-linda-thomas-greenfield-at-a-sudan-anniversary-public-engagement/) evidence indicating that women and girls as young as fourteen years old have been victims of sexual violence perpetrated by the RSF.  

Humanitarian access remains a crucial concern for many international actors, including the [United States](https://usun.usmission.gov/remarks-at-the-un-security-council-stakeout-announcing-additional-humanitarian-assistance-for-sudan-and-neighboring-countries/), which called on the UN Security Council to authorize aid deliveries through Chad. [Conditions in the country](https://www.cfr.org/in-brief/what-extent-sudans-humanitarian-crisis) were already poor before April 2023 and have worsened since. Over six hundred people [died](https://apnews.com/article/sudan-death-toll-un-fighting-khartoum-de76a27db2d1f871430142d172829f6f) in the first month of fighting, and attacks have [destroyed hospitals](https://www.usaid.gov/news-information/press-releases/may-04-2023-attacks-health-workers-and-facilities-worsen-dire-humanitarian-situation-sudan) and other vital infrastructure. In August 2023, the United Nations stated that the conflict in Sudan was [“spiraling out of control”](https://www.cnn.com/2023/08/16/africa/sudan-one-million-flee-un-intl/index.html#:~:text=Sudan%20civil%20war%20'spiraling%20out,more%20than%201%20million%20flee&text=More%20than%20one%20million%20people,the%20country%20into%20civil%20war.) as refugees continued to flee the country and the health system collapsed, raising fears of [disease outbreaks](https://reliefweb.int/report/sudan/who-scales-response-following-sudan-declaration-cholera-outbreak). The displacement crisis is especially concerning given the instability of its bordering countries. In consequence, the UN Humanitarian and Emergency Relief chief [dubbed](https://abcnews.go.com/International/sudan-now-worst-humanitarian-nightmares-recent-history/story?id=104173197) Sudan “one of the worst humanitarian nightmares in recent history.”  

On March 8, 2024, the UN Security Council (UNSC) [passed](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/un-urges-sudan-truce-russia-china-cite-gaza-2024-03-08/) a resolution calling for an immediate cessation of violence in Sudan. A few days later, the SAF agreed to [indirect negotiations](https://www.al-monitor.com/originals/2024/03/sudans-army-ready-indirect-talks-hemedtis-rsf-libya-turkey) with the RSF, mediated by Libya and Turkey. However, the talks broke down on March 11 after a top SAF general [rejected](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/sudan-army-general-rules-out-ramadan-truce-unless-rsf-leaves-civilian-sites-2024-03-10/) the proposal for a ceasefire unless RSF forces withdrew from civilian sites. The statement came after the SAF made [significant advances](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/sudans-army-chief-vows-push-after-advance-embattled-capital-2024-03-13/) toward recapturing Khartoum. Iranian [armed drones](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/world/iran-drones-sudan-civil-war-army-rapid-support-forces-army-rcna147138) partially contributed to the SAF’s successes.  

## Recent Developments  
In the latter half of 2024, the SAF launched a [coordinated offensive](https://acleddata.com/2024/10/14/turning-the-tide-the-safs-strategic-offensive-in-khartoum-and-the-rsfs-setbacks-sudan-october-2024/) around the three metropolitan cities of Khartoum, Omdurman, and Bahri, catching RSF forces off guard. The SAF seized significant territory in the capital for the first time since the RSF took control at the onset of the war. As 2025 began, fighting in Khartoum state intensified, with the SAF retaking key areas around the capital. In January, the SAF [drove RSF forces out](https://www.crisisgroup.org/africa/horn-africa/sudan/battle-khartoum-marks-crossroads-sudans-civil-war) of Omdurman, recaptured a vital oil refinery just north of Khartoum, and [regained](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/2/8/sudan-army-claims-major-advances-against-rsf-in-greater-khartoum) near-total control of Bahri. Additionally, in February 2025, SAF forces [ended](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/ce8ve60r53yo) the RSF’s two-year siege of Obeid, a strategic city with railway connections to Khartoum.  

In February 2025, RSF leadership and its allies gathered in Nairobi, Kenya, to advance their plan to [create a parallel government](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/sudans-rsf-allied-groups-sign-charter-form-parallel-government-two-signatories-2025-02-22/). They signed a charter outlining key aspects of a post-war government, including secularism, democracy, a decentralized structure, and a unified national army. In early March, the RSF [signed a new constitution](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/sudans-rsf-allies-formalise-vision-parallel-government-2025-03-04/), signaling its intent to garner diplomatic leverage and legitimacy. That same month, the Sudanese government [filed a complaint](https://apnews.com/article/sudan-war-icj-uae-8b27cb3b8e4fef5b05a6b45084c967c7) to the International Court of Justice, accusing the UAE of complicity in genocide due to its arms support for the RSF.  

The RSF has continued its attacks on local rebel forces, SAF personnel, and civilians to consolidate its control of Darfur in western Sudan. The RSF [captured El Fasher](https://news.un.org/en/story/2025/10/1166224), the capital of North Darfur and the last major city under government control, on October 27 after an eighteen-month siege. In the following days, reports of [widespread RSF atrocities](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/11/3/new-light-shed-on-el-fasher-horror-as-survivors-arrive-in-sudans-tawila) began to surface, including cases of mass killings, sexual violence, and other crimes. The RSF’s targeting of non-Arab ethnic groups in Darfur has raised concerns of [genocide](https://www.dw.com/en/sudan-mass-killings-prompt-fears-of-a-new-darfur-genocide/a-74546901). According to a [report from Yale’s Humanitarian Research Lab](https://files-profile.medicine.yale.edu/documents/876b4afc-e1da-495b-ac32-b5098699a371), satellite imagery has detected closely packed objects and ground discoloration, which researchers believe to be evidence of human bodies. Reports of famine are exacerbating the country’s humanitarian situation, as nearly [four hundred thousand people](https://www.npr.org/2025/11/03/g-s1-96318/famine-spreads-to-two-more-areas-in-sudan-global-hunger-authority-says) facing starvation.
