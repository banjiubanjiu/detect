**Data Cutoff: March 26, 2026**

*The China & Taiwan Update is a joint product from the Institute for the Study of War (ISW) and the American Enterprise Institute (AEI). The update supports the ISW–AEI Coalition Defense of Taiwan project, which assesses Chinese campaigns against Taiwan, examines alternative strategies for the United States and its allies to deter the Chinese Communist Party’s (CCP) aggression, and—if necessary—defeat the People’s Liberation Army. The update focuses on the CCP’s paths to controlling Taiwan and cross–Taiwan Strait developments.*

## Toplines

**The US Office of the Director of National Intelligence (ODNI) released its 2026 Annual Threat Assessment report on March 18 and assessed that the PRC likely will not invade Taiwan in 2027, but would continue coercive action around Taiwan and the Indo-Pacific. **ODNI’s annual threat assessment summarizes the most direct, serious threats facing the United States as assessed by the US Intelligence Community (IC). ODNI stated that the IC “assesses that Chinese leaders do not currently plan to execute an invasion of Taiwan in 2027 nor do they have a fixed timeline for achieving unification” but that the PRC will likely continue to set conditions for unification in 2026.[1] The 2027 invasion window, also known as the “Davidson Window,” was put forth by Admiral Phil Davidson during a 2021 Congressional hearing.[2] Davidson notably was referencing the year by which CCP General Secretary Xi Jinping instructed the PLA to “be ready” to invade Taiwan.[3] ODNI’s assessment aligns with previous ISW-CDOT assessments that the PRC may use short-of-war coercive measures including narrative, economic, and political warfare, to force Taiwan to agree to “reunification” without a PRC invasion.[4] PRC Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA) spokesperson Lin Jian addressed ODNI’s report in a March 19 press conference and told the United States to “exercise caution” in its rhetoric and actions toward Taiwan.[5]

The ODNI report similarly noted PRC efforts to strengthen its presence in the South China Sea, “particularly at the Philippine-claimed Scarborough Reef and Second Thomas Shoal, through persistent military and coast guard patrols and diplomatic and legal actions.”[6] It noted PRC efforts to establish a nature reserve at Scarborough Shoal, a line of effort that ISW-CDOT previously assessed as a coercive action likely intended to increase the PRC’s legal cover for aggressive actions against Philippine vessels.[7]

**The US IC assessed that the PRC likely views Japan’s stance on the defense of Taiwan as an inflection point. Japan is continuing to insulate itself from PRC coercion efforts, partially through cooperation with the United States. **ODNI’s report stated that Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s statement suggesting potential Japanese involvement in a Taiwan contingency resulted in increased PRC-Japan tensions.[8] ODNI emphasized that the PRC “probably is concerned that Prime Minister Takaichi’s comments will bolster Taiwan’s independence movement.”[9] Japanese Chief Cabinet Secretary Minoru Kihara on March 19 expressed disagreement with ODNI’s assessment, stating that “the claim of a significant policy shift is inaccurate” and that Japan’s position on Taiwan had “always been consistent.”[10] The ODNI report also highlighted the PRC’s retaliatory measures against Japan, including flight cancellations and import restrictions, following Takaichi’s comments.[11] ODNI noted that the PRC would likely escalate its coercive actions around the Senkaku Islands to show its disagreement with Japan.[12]

US President Donald Trump met with Takaichi on March 19 to discuss security and industrial cooperation. The White House framed Trump’s discussions with Takaichi as a renewal of US-Japan ties, highlighting joint security cooperation efforts without directly referencing the PRC. Trump and Takaichi affirmed their commitment to “peace and stability across the Taiwan Strait” and the denuclearization of North Korea.[13] The White House readout emphasized Japan’s efforts to “strengthen its mechanism for reviewing inbound investment based on national security risks” – likely a reference to Japan’s version of the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS), an investigatory body aimed at preventing foreign investment risks.[14] The White House readout similarly emphasized that the United States and Japan would continue working to address “challenges posed by strategic competitors and rogue states,” likely an indirect reference to the PRC.[15] The United States and Japan pledged to deepen industrial and defense cooperation, with the White House readout referencing the deployment of US Typhon missiles – a major point of contention with the PRC – to Japan.[16] PRC state media outlet *Xinhua* on March 21 accused Takaichi of “flattering” diplomacy and framing Takaichi’s discussion with Trump as humiliating.[17] PRC media outlet *Global Times* on March 23 ran a similar editorial framing the US-Japan talks as illustrative of Japanese “dependency” on the United States.[18]

PRC-Japan tensions have continued to escalate since Trump and Takaichi’s meeting.[19] The PRC consistently ascribes the tensions to Takaichi’s comments and has imposed increasing economic pressure to force Japan to retract its statement.[20] Japanese business leaders were reportedly excluded from the PRC’s list of attendees at the China Development Forum, an annual economic forum held in Beijing.[21] Japan plans to downgrade its ties with the PRC from “one of its most important” relationships, according to a draft of Japan’s 2026 Diplomatic Bluebook.[22] Lin Jian again blamed Takaichi’s comments on Taiwan as the origin of PRC-Japan tensions when asked about this diplomatic downgrade during a March 24 press conference.[23]

**The PRC may rely on the PLA Navy’s (PLAN) Type 055 guided missile destroyers (DDG) to conduct air defense missions during a conflict with the United States. The PRC is likely emphasizing an air defense role for the Type 055 in light of US military successes in Venezuela and Iran. **PRC state broadcasting service CCTV released footage on March 21 of the Type 055 DDG *Nanchang* conducting live-fire training focusing on air and missile defense.[24] PRC state media site *Global Times* reported on the footage claiming that the HHQ-9 (the Type 055’s principal anti-air missile) has a range of “several hundred” kilometers, allowing the vessel to conduct “area air defense” missions.[25] PRC state media outlet *Xinhua* highlighted on March 20 the Type 055’s advanced radar system that can track multiple targets, guide munitions for precision interceptions, and direct fires from other platforms.[26] *PLA Daily* similarly described on March 22 a training exercise of Type 055 DDGs *Dongguan* and *Anqing* that focused on aerial interception of aircraft and missiles within a “complex electromagnetic environment.”[27]

The United States Air Force and Navy field several assets that possess electronic warfare capabilities and anti-radiation missiles that it used during operations against Venezuela and Iran to suppress air defense networks and rapidly gain control of the sky.[28] The PRC’s mention of operating under a “complex electromagnetic environment” likely indicates it is attempting to rehearse procedures to overcome a similar challenge. Integrating the Type 055 into the PRC’s overall air defense network would provide the PRC another layer of air defenses that neither Iran nor Venezuela possessed, potentially complicating US efforts to establish lasting air superiority in the Indo-Pacific Theater. The PRC may also be emphasizing the air defense capabilities of the Type 055 to create the impression that the PRC does not have the same vulnerabilities as Iran or Venezuela.

The PLAN’s reliance on the Type 055 to perform air defense roles may limit its operations beyond the first island chain. The PLAN’s carriers would likely operate beyond the first island chain during a blockade or invasion of Taiwan to intercept US intervention efforts.[29] An air defense role would prevent at least some portion of the PLAN’s 10 Type 055s from escorting PLAN aircraft carriers while they conduct missions in the Western Pacific, significantly limiting the combat power of a PLAN carrier strike group.[30] The United States military can, as a result, impose dilemmas for the PLAN with its air assets that could force it to remain within the first island chain and be incapable of interdicting US support for Taiwan.

## Cross-Strait Relations

### Taiwan

**Taiwan charged an opposition Taiwan People’s Party (TPP) campaigner for violating Taiwan’s Anti-Infiltration Act and acting on behalf of PRC handlers. The PRC is likely seeking to influence Taiwan’s upcoming 2026 and 2028 elections and promote anti-DPP candidates.** Xu Chunying, a PRC immigrant to Taiwan, campaigned on behalf of TPP candidates, including former TPP chairman Ko Wen-je, after PRC handlers recruited her in the PRC.[31] Xu chaired the Taiwan New Immigrants Development Association and traveled to the PRC under the pretext of assisting PRC spouses in Taiwan. Taiwanese prosecutors alleged that Xu expressed allegiance to the CCP.[32] Xu’s case centered on “mainland spouses” living in Taiwan, similar to efforts by convicted Taiwanese resident Zhou Manzhi.[33] Zhou was born in the PRC and similarly led civil organizations that targeted PRC spouses married to Taiwanese citizens, attempting to recruit them to support the CCP.[34] The PRC likely seeks to reduce the influence of the DPP, which has pushed for defense reforms that the PRC perceives as threatening.

Election interference provides the PRC with a non-kinetic means of influencing Taiwanese policy. Taiwanese research organization DoubleThink Labs reported that the PRC is compiling information likely intended to influence Taiwanese public opinion surrounding upcoming local and presidential elections.[35] The PRC also employed a range of political warfare tactics to influence Taiwan’s 2024 elections.[36] Taiwan explored amending the Anti-Infiltration Act in January 2026, with some legislators pushing for harsher sentences for PRC-linked infiltrations.[37]

**Taiwan’s National Defense Committee and Finance Committee held a joint hearing from March 23 to 26 to decide between three competing special defense bills, but was unable to reach a conclusion. Some opposition Kuomintang (KMT) lawmakers advocated that a new special defense bill should replace the KMT’s current version, indicating a possible split in the KMT. T**he Finance Committee and National Defense Committee referred the three competing budgets, from the Executive Yuan (EY), Taiwan People’s Party (TPP), and KMT to cross-party negotiations for one month after failing to reach a majority consensus.[38] The KMT’s budget amounts to 380 billion New Taiwan Dollars (NTD) and is comparable to the TPP’s budget of 400 billion NTD. The opposition budgets are less than a third of the original EY version of 1.25 trillion NTD. The KMT’s budget funds the purchase of eight US weapons systems: Javelin missiles, TOW 2B missiles, M109A7, ALTIUS 600 and 700M, Harpoon missile support, helicopter parts, and the construction of a tactical mission net.[39] Director of the Strategic Planning Department of Taiwan’s Ministry of National Defense Huang Wen-chi said Taiwan’s failure to pass a special defense budget could delay the purchase of the US systems until the end of 2026.[40]

Multiple current and former KMT legislators and politicians have voiced support for a new KMT special defense act with a spending cap of 810 billion NTD, indicating possible division in KMT voting on the defense budget. The details of this alternate KMT special defense budget have not been released as of time of writing. The alternate KMT defense budget has not officially been introduced to Taiwan’s Legislative Yuan and the relevant committees. Disagreement between KMT legislators on the optimal defense budget could lead to continued difficulties passing the special defense budget, thus leaving Taiwan unable to purchase necessary military equipment.

### China

**The US Department of Justice (DOJ) held press releases on two court cases involving individuals smuggling advanced artificial intelligence (AI) technologies to the PRC through Southeast Asian intermediaries in violation of US export control laws. **The US DOJ unsealed an indictment on March 19 charging three individuals affiliated with server manufacturing company Super Micro for diverting servers containing advanced chips to the PRC worth at least hundreds of millions of US Dollars (USD) in violation of US export-control laws. The three men are Super Micro co-founder and senior vice president of business development Yih-Shyan “Wally” Liaw, Super Micro Taiwan office manager Ruei-Tsang Chang, and outside contractor Ting-Wei Sun. [41] The US DOJ charged them with one count of conspiring to smuggle goods from the United States and one count of conspiring to defraud the United States.[42] The US DOJ accused the three individuals of diverting servers containing advanced Nvidia chips to the PRC by sending them to a company based in Southeast Asia that posed as the end user. The Southeast Asian company then repackaged the servers into unmarked boxes sent to the PRC.[43] The intermediary company falsified records and used nonworking server copies to deceive auditors. The intermediary company purchased 2.5 billion USD worth of servers from Super Micro between 2024 and 2025.[44] *Reuters* reported in 2024 that the PRC was able to acquire high-end Nvidia chips embedded in Super Micro server products.[45]

The US DOJ announced on March 25 that it charged three individuals with “conspiring to commit smuggling and export control violations,” who sought to route millions of dollars worth of chips through Thailand into the PRC.[46] These individuals, similar to the Super Micro case, used the names of Southeast Asian companies as the purported end user on official paperwork. These two cases demonstrate that PRC entities are still able to acquire advanced chips from the United States through foreign intermediaries.

US Senators Jim Banks and Elizabeth Warren wrote a letter to US Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick calling for “the immediate pausing, suspension, or other reconsideration of all active export licenses” for advanced Nvidia chips and server products to the PRC and Southeast Asian intermediaries in response to this case.[47] The *Financial Times* reported that many PRC companies build data centers in Southeast Asia to access advanced chips necessary to train their AI models.[48] This allows for the PRC to bypass export control restrictions as Southeast Asian countries are not banned from accessing advanced chips from the United States, unlike the PRC. The US-China Economic and Security Review Commission reported that the PRC narrowed performance gaps with Western large language models despite technological limitations, and that the PRC’s open development model is increasing compute efficiency and thus making it “increasingly independent of access to cutting-edge hardware.”[49]

## Northeast Asia

### Japan

**The Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force (JMSDF) overhauled its structure to facilitate responding to PRC aggression against the Senkaku and Ryukyu islands. The PRC will likely frame the restructuring as Japanese aggression or militarism despite Japan’s defensive intentions. **The JMSDF replaced its fleet escort force with a fleet surface force on March 23 to centralize assets and capabilities.[50] The reform decreases the number of surface groups in the JMSDF from four to three, increasing the number of vessels within each grouping of forces.[51] The three force groupings will be based in Yokosuka, Kure, and Maizuru.[52] The JMSDF also created an information warfare and operations command to centralize its intelligence and cyber assets.[53] The JMSDF also established an amphibious and mine warfare group.[54]

The reforms are largely centered on to countering PRC aggression against Japan’s Senkaku and Ryukyu islands, which have strategic significance for a Taiwan contingency. The new amphibious and mine warfare group will be headquartered in Sasebo, proximate to Japan’s southwestern islands and the headquarters of the Japan Ground Self-Defense Force’s (JGSDF) Amphibious Rapid Deployment Brigade.[55] The new amphibious formation will allow the Japan Self-Defense Forces (JSDF) to rapidly deploy assets and personnel to the Senkaku and Ryukyu islands during a conflict with the PRC. The PRC officially claims the Senkaku Islands as its own and has conducted an information operation to suggest that the Ryukyu Islands should assert its independence from Japan.[56] The PLAN must pass through the Senkaku and Ryukyu islands via the Miyako Strait to operate beyond the first island chain.[57] The JSDF could constrain the PLAN’s ability to deploy assets beyond the first island chain in wartime by strengthening defenses around these islands, complicating PLAN efforts to interdict overseas support for Taiwan.

PRC state media outlet *Global Times* commented on the reforms on March 23 that claimed they provided Japan a “pretext” to “target” the PRC.[58] The commentary likewise claimed that the force restructuring was evidence that Japan was abandoning its post-WWII self-defense posture.[59] The PRC frequently frames Japanese military modernization as aggressive to dissuade Japan from enhancing its ability to counter PRC regional aggression.[60]

## Southeast Asia

### Philippines

**The Philippine Navy’s (PN) improved surface fleet may enhance Philippine efforts to resist PRC coercion in the South China Sea. PN surface fleet improvements could force the PRC to allocate more advanced resources to the region, potentially straining its ability to exert influence in the Western Pacific. **The PN announced on March 18 that PLAN Type 056A corvette *Guang’an* locked its fire control radar onto the Philippine guided missile frigate (FFG) *Miguel Malvar *on March 7 near Sabina Shoal and within the Philippines’ exclusive economic zone (EEZ).[61] The PN claimed *Miguel Malvar* issued a radio challenge to *Guang’an* that caused it to stow its fire control radar.[62] The PRC claims sovereignty over Sabina Shoal, and ISW-CDOT has assessed that the China Coast Guard (CCG) has increased its presence around the shoal in 2025 to deny the Philippines use of the island as a staging ground for resupply missions in the South China Sea.[63]

The outcome of the confrontation suggests that modern PN assets can withstand coercive measures from lower-end PLAN vessels. *Miguel Malvar* is the Philippines’ first purpose-built surface vessel, designed in South Korea and equipped with modern anti-ship and anti-air armaments.[64] The Philippines has traditionally relied on acquiring retired vessels from its allies and has remained behind the cutting edge of naval technology.[65] The PLAN has previously used the fire control radar on the Type 056A to intimidate PN vessels and refused to cease targeting a Philippine corvette when challenged in the Spratly Islands in February 2020.[66] *Miguel Malvar’s* challenge caused the PLAN corvette to stow its radar, potentially indicating that the more modern Philippine vessel could withstand PLAN coercive measures. The PN is aiming to deploy a total of four *Miguel Malvar*-class FFGs by 2028 as part of its broader military modernization plan.[67] The *Miguel Malvar*-class in particular highlights the benefits of Philippine security cooperation with South Korea in the maritime domain.

Increasing the number of modern surface combatants the PN can deploy to the South China Sea could force the PRC to reevaluate its coercion calculus in the region and strain its high-end resources by forcing them to cover multiple lines of effort. The Type 056A corvette is a second-rate PLAN vessel that it has likely allocated to the South China Sea because the PRC sees the PN as a second-rate competitor.[68] Fielding modern vessels that outclass PLAN corvettes may lead the PRC to deploy higher-end vessels to achieve its goals in the region. The PRC has a limited number of these assets, however, and a more capable Philippine surface fleet could cause the PLAN to become overstretched and have to deploy high-end assets against the Philippines, Taiwan, and Japan to maintain its regional military presence.

**The PRC fired flares at a Type C-208 Philippine aircraft flying near the South China Sea feature Mischief Reef on March 20. The Philippines and the United States conducted a bilateral maritime cooperative activity (MCA) in Manila Bay and waters off Mariveles, Bataan on the same day. **Spokesperson for the PLA Southern Theater Command (STC) Senior Colonel Zhai Shichen stated that the PLA organized naval and air forces to track, monitor, warn, and drive away the aircraft.[69] The PRC sent radio challenges and fired flares at the aircraft, PCG *Cessna Caravan*, while the aircraft was conducting a maritime domain awareness flight (MDA). Philippine Coast Guard (PCG) spokesperson Rear Admiral Jay Tarriela was on board the plane along with local officials, journalists, and PCG personnel.[70] Tarriela posted to X (formerly Twitter) that the PRC has been illegally reclaiming Mischief Reef, which lies within the Philippine exclusive economic zone (EEZ).[71] Mischief Reef is about 120 nautical miles from the Palawan coast and over 600 nautical miles from the PRC.[72] The PRC built a military facility on the reef, and satellite imagery shows that the base possesses anti-aircraft guns, a 3000-meter runway, radar systems, and more.[73]

The Philippine-US MCA, dubbed Bilateral MCA 26-3.1, was a separate event from the Mischief Reef altercation and is the fourth US MCA of 2026.[74] The exercise focused on communication drills and interoperability between the Armed Forces of the Philippines, US Indo-Pacific Command, and the PCG.[75] This marked the US Navy’s 7th Fleet flagship USS *Blue Ridge’s* first MCA participation.[76] US 7th Fleet Commander Vice Admiral Patrick J. Hannifin visited the PCG headquarters prior to the MCA, the first visit by a US Navy fleet commander, where he met with PCG Commandant Admiral Ronnie Gil Gavan.[77]
