# Democratic Republic of Congo

_In 2025, violence in the eastern DR Congo has increased dramatically. The Rwanda-backed M23 rebels took over Goma, a provincial capital, in late January after heavy fighting costing thousands of lives. Shortly afterward, they captured another key city, Bukavu, and they have since continued to expand to the south and west. Hundreds of thousands of civilians have fled their homes as fighting spreads, with women and families bearing the brunt of displacement. The conflict has deepened divisions among neighbouring countries, particularly between Rwanda and Burundi. Since Angola withdrew from its African Union-mandated mediation role in mid-March, attempts including by the U.S. and Qatar to get the various warring sides to meaningfully engage have faltered. Efforts culminated in an agreement signed in Washington on 4 December, though this did little to tamp down the fighting. The national picture is fast-moving as well. Opposition politicians have rejected President Félix Tshisekedi’s offer of a national union government. Relations were already prickly following poorly run elections in 2023, and Tshisekedi’s subsequent attempt to initiate a change to the constitution, which his opponents suspect hides a desire to stay in power beyond his two-term limit. Crisis Group aims to provide detailed recommendations for how to calm national tensions and de-escalate the situation in the east._

**Drone warfare signalled a new phase in the eastern DRC conflict, increasing the threat on civilians and infrastructure in urban areas and outside the Kivu provinces, while a new armed group emerged in the Haut-Katanga province.**

_Drone warfare intensified amid continued conflict in east despite tentative M23 withdrawals_. Govt forces and allied Wazalendo militias continued to clash with Rwandan forces and M23 armed group across North Kivu, notably in Masisi and Walikale territories, and in South Kivu’s Uvira and Minembwe areas, with both sides increasingly relying on drone warfare. In North Kivu, army drone strike reportedly targeting ex-President Kabila 11 March hit regional capital Goma, killing three civilians. Drone strikes also reported around 10 March in South Kivu’s Minembwe highlands (see Burundi). Conflict spilled beyond Kivus into Tshopo province as M23 drone strike 1 March targeted Kisangani airport for second time in five days. Meanwhile, Washington 2 March sanctioned Rwandan army for supporting M23 and 17-18 March hosted talks between Kinshasa and Kigali in bid to revive implementation of Dec 2025 Washington Peace Accords (see Rwanda). M23 forces 23-24 March reportedly started withdrawing from several villages in North Kivu’s Lubero territory, but clashes erupted in following days as Wazalendo allegedly attempted to re-enter villages.

_New armed activity emerged in Haut-Katanga province_. New armed group Mouvement Debout Katanga pour la Libération du Congo 3 March launched attacks on villages in Mitwaba-Lusinga area and announced armed campaign against govt, invoking local grievances while also criticising national governance. Authorities reportedly deployed troops to Mitwaba territory, suggesting concern over risk of escalation – including, potentially, coordination with other armed groups – and territorial entrenchment in mineral-rich Katanga.

_Armed groups continued to wreak havoc in Ituri province._ Islamic State-affiliated Allied Democratic Forces (ADF) 9-19 March killed at least 60 people in Mambasa territory, and 28-29 March reportedly abducted dozens along Mambasa-Komanda road. Uganda’s expanding military presence in Ituri fuelled local tensions, with some residents accusing Kampala – whose troops conduct joint operations with Congolese forces against ADF – of backing International Criminal Court convict Thomas Lubanga’s militia. In sign of growing tension, provincial authorities 4 March moved to restrict entry of Ugandan troops at Mahagi border crossing.
