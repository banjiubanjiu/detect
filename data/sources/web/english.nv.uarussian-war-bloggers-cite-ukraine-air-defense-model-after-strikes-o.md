# Russian oil strikes expose air defense strain as war bloggers warn of missile shortages— ISW

April 7, 2026, 07:49 AM

Author: [Alex Stezhensky](https://english.nv.ua/korrespondents/alex-stezhensky.html)

Ukraine is expanding its long-range strike campaign against Russian oil infrastructure as Russian war bloggers complain their own air defenses are ineffective and unable to limit the damage from those attacks, the Institute for the Study of War said in a [report](https://understandingwar.org/research/russia-ukraine/russian-offensive-campaign-assessment-april-6-2026/) on April 6.

ISW analysts said Ukraine’s latest strikes on the Sheskharis oil terminal and the frigate Admiral Makarov in the port of Novorossiysk expand a campaign that is already significantly undermining Russia’s oil export capacity.

The Institute for the Study of War said Russia’s geographic scale “creates a substantial challenge” for defending such targets, especially with the traditional air defense systems that Russian forces still appear to rely on against Ukrainian drone attacks. Analysts also noted that Ukraine had previously carried out a large-scale campaign against the radars of Russian air defense systems. As a result, Russia’s air defense network is under strain as it tries to cover vast territory.

Against that backdrop, Russian war bloggers are criticizing the ineffectiveness of Russian air defense systems and emphasizing the impact of Ukrainian strikes, ISW said.

One Kremlin-linked war blogger said on April 6 that constant Ukrainian strikes were causing Russian air defense units to “wear out” while their missile stocks were being depleted at an accelerated pace. He also complained that “it is physically impossible to simply create tens of thousands of missiles for Pantsir [air defense systems] out of thin air.”

He criticized proposals in Russian media calling for more guided anti-aircraft missiles and air defense systems to protect against Ukrainian strikes. “For some reason, no one is trying to understand how difficult it is to simply increase production of this type of weapon,” the war blogger complained.

Instead, he urged the Russian military to copy Ukraine’s approach to countering aerial attacks — “more mobile fire groups, more interceptor drone crews, more acoustic reconnaissance systems.”

ISW added that other Russian war bloggers are also complaining that recovery efforts after Ukrainian strikes consume resources, while daytime attacks by Ukrainian drones put additional strain on Russian air defenses. They also acknowledge the systematic nature of Ukraine’s long-range drone strikes and the vulnerability of Russian warships stationed at bases within range of Ukrainian drones.

ISW analysts concluded that Russia still appears either not to have fully developed or not yet to have deployed a system of mobile fire groups, interceptor drones or other low-cost defenses against repeated Ukrainian strikes on widely dispersed targets inside Russia.

There have been reports that the Kremlin has allowed private businesses to develop their own — likely low-cost — counterdrone approaches. However, the targets recently struck by Ukraine’s Defense Forces do not appear to have been adequately protected by such methods, ISW said.

ISW assessed that Ukraine’s large-scale long-range strike campaign, which is already significantly undermining Russian oil exports, will likely also force Russian troops to divert valuable manpower and materiel to air defense rather than deploy those resources against Ukraine.

“Russian forces are likely to adapt over time, given their initial attempts to develop interceptor drones and discussions about forming mobile fire groups, but the need to defend many large, widely dispersed facilities may remain a difficult challenge for them,” ISW said.

Overnight into April 7, drones again attacked the port of Ust-Luga in Russia’s Leningrad Oblast after reports that it had partially resumed operations.

Overnight into April 6, Ukraine’s Defense Forces also carried out successful strikes on the Sheskharis oil terminal in Novorossiysk. The General Staff of Ukraine’s Armed Forces said a large fire broke out at the terminal, and the Russian frigate Admiral Makarov was also hit there.

According to NV sources in Ukraine’s SBU Security Service, six of the terminal’s seven oil-loading berths used to load and unload oil from tankers were damaged in the strike. Hits were also recorded on the terminal’s on-site infrastructure: a key junction in the pipeline system and an oil metering hub were struck, and large fires broke out at the impact sites.
