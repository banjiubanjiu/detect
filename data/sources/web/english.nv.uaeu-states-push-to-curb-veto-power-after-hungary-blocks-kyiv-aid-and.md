# EU states push to curb veto power as Hungary stalls Kyiv aid and Russia action

April 7, 2026, 02:33 PM

A growing group of EU countries wants to rein in — or even eliminate — the power of a single member state to block foreign and security policy decisions, [Politico](https://www.politico.eu/article/eu-foreign-policy-shambles-triggers-calls-for-radical-overhaul-of-diplomacy/) reported on April 7, as frustration mounts over repeated holdups on aid for Kyiv and action against Russia.

The outlet said the push to revisit unanimity reflects the EU’s repeated failure to act with one voice — including on unlocking a 90 billion euro loan for Ukraine and approving measures targeting Russia.

Germany and Sweden are leading the effort to sharply limit national veto power, which currently allows any one country to stop joint action by the bloc.

“We should abolish the principle of unanimity in the EU in foreign and security policy by the end of the current legislative period, so that we are better able to act internationally and truly come of age,” Politico quoted German Foreign Minister Johann Wadephul as saying.

“All the experience we have gained in recent weeks with aid to Ukraine and sanctions against Russia points to that,” Germany’s top diplomat said on April 5.

Swedish Prime Minister Ulf Kristersson has also said EU leaders are likely to return to discussions about using qualified majority voting in foreign policy.

The debate has intensified after Hungary repeatedly blocked major EU decisions, including the 90 billion euro loan to Kyiv. Member states are increasingly worried that the bloc’s foreign policy is being shaped — or stalled — by domestic political calculations in a single country.

Diplomats told Politico that even if Hungarian Prime Minister Viktor Orbán loses the parliamentary election on April 12, the underlying problem will remain, because unanimity would still give any future government the same power to obstruct.

“There are serious problems in the way we make decisions,” Spanish lawmaker Nacho Sánchez Amor, a member of the European Parliament’s Foreign Affairs Committee, told Politico. “Every month brings a new issue that proves it convincingly. We must respond.”

Still, some countries — including France and Belgium — are firmly defending the veto, arguing that it remains essential to protecting national interests.

“I believe that starting a debate now about the rules of European unanimity would be the shortest path to serious problems,” Belgian Prime Minister Bart De Wever said in March.

On April 3, EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas said the bloc’s institutions need to “function as one team.”

“No one distinguishes whether something comes from the European Commission, the European External Action Service or the Council of the European Union,” Kallas said in an interview with Estonian broadcaster ERR.
