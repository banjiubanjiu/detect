[Myanmar](/wiki/Myanmar "Myanmar") has been in [armed conflict](/wiki/Armed_conflict "Armed conflict") since 1948, when the country, then known as Burma, [gained independence](/wiki/Burmese_Declaration_of_Independence "Burmese Declaration of Independence") from the United Kingdom.[[5]](#cite_note-8)[[6]](#cite_note-9) The conflict has largely been [ethnic-based](/wiki/Ethnic_conflict "Ethnic conflict"), with [ethnic armed organisations](/wiki/List_of_ethnic_armed_organisations "List of ethnic armed organisations") fighting Myanmar's armed forces, the [Tatmadaw](/wiki/Tatmadaw "Tatmadaw"), for [self-determination](/wiki/Self-determination "Self-determination"). Despite [numerous ceasefires](/wiki/Ceasefires_in_Myanmar "Ceasefires in Myanmar") and the creation of autonomous [self-administered zones](/wiki/Self-administered_zone "Self-administered zone") in 2008, armed groups continue to call for independence, [increased autonomy](/wiki/Autonomous_administrative_division "Autonomous administrative division"), or the [federalisation of Myanmar](/wiki/Federalism_in_Myanmar "Federalism in Myanmar"). It is the world's longest ongoing civil war, spanning almost eight decades.[[7]](#cite_note-10)[[8]](#cite_note-11)[[9]](#cite_note-12)

In 1940, during [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), Burmese intellectuals formed the [Thirty Comrades](/wiki/Thirty_Comrades "Thirty Comrades"), who established the [Burma Independence Army](/wiki/Burma_Independence_Army "Burma Independence Army") (BIA) to fight against [the Allies](/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II "Allies of World War II"). [Aung San](/wiki/Aung_San "Aung San") led the [Axis](/wiki/Axis_powers "Axis powers")-puppet [State of Burma](/wiki/State_of_Burma "State of Burma"), before switching sides to the Allies in mid-1944. Post-war negotiations led to Burma's independence in 1948, but ethnic tensions arose after the Burmese government refused to honour the 1947 [Panglong Agreement](/wiki/Panglong_Agreement "Panglong Agreement"), which promised autonomy for some of the country's ethnic minorities. The immediate post-independence period saw the rise of the [Communist Party of Burma](/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Burma "Communist Party of Burma") (CPB) and [Karen National Union](/wiki/Karen_National_Union "Karen National Union") (KNU) in particular as major rebel forces.

In 1962, Burmese general [Ne Win](/wiki/Ne_Win "Ne Win") led a military coup, establishing a junta and refusing to adopt a federal system of governance, which led to intensified insurgencies. Ne Win's regime faced internal dissent and growing civil conflict throughout his rule, culminating in the [8888 Uprising](/wiki/8888_Uprising "8888 Uprising") in 1988, which was violently suppressed by the military. Following the uprising, the military established the State Law and Order Restoration Council (SLORC), later renamed the [State Peace and Development Council](/wiki/State_Peace_and_Development_Council "State Peace and Development Council") (SPDC).

Civilian rule was restored in 2011, albeit not fully, with the military retaining power in the country's legislatures through a new constitution. A [military coup in 2021](/wiki/2021_Myanmar_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2021 Myanmar coup d'état") by commander-in-chief [Min Aung Hlaing](/wiki/Min_Aung_Hlaing "Min Aung Hlaing") deposed the civilian government, sparking [widespread protests](/wiki/Myanmar_protests_(2021%E2%80%93present) "Myanmar protests (2021–present)") and [escalating insurgencies](/wiki/Myanmar_civil_war_(2021%E2%80%93present) "Myanmar civil war (2021–present)").

Prelude
-------

In 1940, during [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), a group of young Burmese intellectuals left for Japan to receive military training in preparation for an anti-colonial struggle against the British.[[10]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199159-13) This group came to be known as the [Thirty Comrades](/wiki/Thirty_Comrades "Thirty Comrades"), and upon returning to Burma in 1941 they established the [Burma Independence Army](/wiki/Burma_Independence_Army "Burma Independence Army") (BIA) to fight against the Allies.[[10]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199159-13)[[11]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHensengerth200510-14) Upon their capture of Rangoon in 1942, the Japanese established a [puppet state](/wiki/Puppet_state "Puppet state"), the [State of Burma](/wiki/State_of_Burma "State of Burma"), and reorganised the BIA as its armed forces, the Burma National Army (BNA).[[12]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199161-15) [Aung San](/wiki/Aung_San "Aung San"), the leader of the State of Burma and one of the Thirty Comrades, became increasingly sceptical of Japan's ability to win the war as time progressed, and in mid-1944 he decided to switch sides.[[13]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199160-16) Japanese forces capitulated by July 1945, and the British began to negotiate Burma's independence with Aung San and other prominent Burmese leaders.[[14]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHensengerth200510–12-17)

In the lead up to Burmese independence, Aung San negotiated with [Chin](/wiki/Chin_people "Chin people"), [Kachin](/wiki/Kachin_people "Kachin people"), and [Shan](/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people") leaders, and the [Panglong Agreement](/wiki/Panglong_Agreement "Panglong Agreement") was reached between them. The agreement promised full autonomy for the areas inhabited by the three ethnic minorities, with an option to secede from Burma ten years after independence.[[15]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199179-18) However, Aung San was [assassinated](/wiki/Aung_San#Assassination "Aung San") shortly afterwards, and the Panglong Agreement was not honoured by the post-independence government under [U Nu](/wiki/U_Nu "U Nu").[[16]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMyint2006274–289-19) This further strained relations between the [Bamar](/wiki/Bamar_people "Bamar people") ethnic majority and the country's many ethnic minorities.[[17]](#cite_note-Maung-20)

Course of the conflict
----------------------

### Post-independence insurgencies (1948–1962)

Main articles: [Communist Party of Burma](/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Burma "Communist Party of Burma"), [Karen conflict § Post-independence](/wiki/Karen_conflict#Post-independence "Karen conflict"), and [Kuomintang in Burma](/wiki/Kuomintang_in_Burma "Kuomintang in Burma")

[![Map of insurgent activity in Burma in 1948](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Burma1948.png/250px-Burma1948.png)](/wiki/File:Burma1948.png)

[![Map of insurgent activity in Burma in 1953](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Burma1953.png/250px-Burma1953.png)](/wiki/File:Burma1953.png)

Map of insurgent activity in Burma in 1948 (top) and 1953 (bottom)

Following Burma's independence from the United Kingdom on 4 January 1948, the two largest opposition groups in the country were the communists, led by the [Communist Party of Burma](/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Burma "Communist Party of Burma") (CPB), and the Karen nationalists, led by the [Karen National Union](/wiki/Karen_National_Union "Karen National Union") (KNU).[[18]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHensengerth200523-21)[[19]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTECallahan2003118–123-22)

Initially there was calm during the transitional period after independence, but on 2 April 1948, the CPB fired the first shots of the conflict in Paukkongyi, Pegu Region (present-day [Bago Region](/wiki/Bago_Region "Bago Region")). At its peak, the CPB had 15,000 fighters in 1949.[[20]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELintner199014-23)

During the post-independence period, the KNU favoured an [independent state](/wiki/Independent_state "Independent state"), governed by the Karen people. The proposed state would have encompassed the territories of Karen State and Karenni State (present-day [Kayin State](/wiki/Kayin_State "Kayin State") and [Kayah State](/wiki/Kayah_State "Kayah State")), in [Lower Burma](/wiki/Lower_Burma "Lower Burma") (Outer Myanmar). The KNU has since shifted their focus from full independence to [regional autonomy](/wiki/Regional_autonomy "Regional autonomy"), under a [federal system](/wiki/Federalism "Federalism") with fair Karen representation in the government.[[21]](#cite_note-KNUabout-24)

### Ne Win's dictatorship (1962–1988)

After three successive parliamentary governments governed Myanmar, the Tatmadaw, led by General [Ne Win](/wiki/Ne_Win "Ne Win"), enacted a [coup d'état on 2 March 1962](/wiki/1962_Burmese_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "1962 Burmese coup d'état"), which ousted the parliamentary government and replaced it with a [military junta](/wiki/Military_junta "Military junta"). Accusations of severe human rights abuses and violations followed afterwards, and the cabinet of the parliamentary government and political leaders of ethnic minority groups were arrested and detained without trial.[[22]](#cite_note-Pavković,_2011:_476-25) Around this period, other ethnic minority groups began forming larger rebel factions, such as the [Kachin Independence Army](/wiki/Kachin_Independence_Army "Kachin Independence Army"), in response to the new government's refusal to adopt a federal system.

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/They_go_back.jpg/250px-They_go_back.jpg)](/wiki/File:They_go_back.jpg)

"They have gone back": Members of the CPB's delegation to the failed 1963 peace talks, returning to their bases by foot, c. November 1963.

Many insurgent groups, communist and ethnonationalist alike, became increasingly receptive of the [Maoist](/wiki/Maoist "Maoist") concept of a "[people's war](/wiki/People%27s_war "People's war")" after failed peace talks with Ne Win's government in 1963. The CPB maintained close relations with the [Chinese Communist Party](/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party "Chinese Communist Party") (CCP) and replicated China's [Cultural Revolution](/wiki/Cultural_Revolution "Cultural Revolution").[[23]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1991229-26)[[24]](#cite_note-27) The CPB's imitation of their Chinese allies was perceived by many Burmese as an attempt by China to intrude into Burmese affairs, a sentiment which led to the violent [1967 anti-Chinese riots in Burma](/wiki/1967_anti-Chinese_riots_in_Burma "1967 anti-Chinese riots in Burma"). By the time the riots were quelled, 31 [Chinese](/wiki/Chinese_people_in_Myanmar "Chinese people in Myanmar") civilians had been killed and several Chinese-owned businesses had been burned down.[[25]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFan2012238-28)

Both immediately after the coup and again in 1972, Ne Win held peace talks with several insurgent groups, but both times they fell apart. This was partly due to Ne Win's refusal to adopt a federal [multi-party system](/wiki/Multi-party_system "Multi-party system").[[26]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTECallahan200334–36-29) After negotiations failed, defectors from the Tatmadaw and ethnic insurgents walked back to their bases, with headlines across Myanmar famously reading "They have gone back" (သူတို့ပြန်ကြလေပြီ). Private property was confiscated by the government, and the [Burmese Socialist Programme Party](/wiki/Burmese_Socialist_Programme_Party "Burmese Socialist Programme Party") (BSPP) was founded in 1974 to govern the country under a [one-party system](/wiki/One-party_state "One-party state"). Under Ne Win's [26-year dictatorship](/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_the_Union_of_Burma "Socialist Republic of the Union of Burma"), Myanmar became an isolated [hermit kingdom](/wiki/Hermit_kingdom "Hermit kingdom") and one of the [least developed countries](/wiki/Human_Development_Index "Human Development Index") in the world. In 1988, [nationwide student protests](/wiki/8888_Uprising "8888 Uprising") resulted in the BSPP and Ne Win being ousted and replaced with a new military regime, the [State Peace and Development Council](/wiki/State_Peace_and_Development_Council "State Peace and Development Council").[[27]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELintner1999-30)[*[page needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")*]

### 8888 Uprising

Main article: [8888 Uprising](/wiki/8888_Uprising "8888 Uprising")

On 12 March 1988, students began demonstrating in Rangoon (present-day Yangon) against the totalitarian rule of Ne Win and his [Burma Socialist Programme Party](/wiki/Burma_Socialist_Programme_Party "Burma Socialist Programme Party") (BSPP).[[28]](#cite_note-31) The protests quickly spread across the country, and the BSPP government was eventually pressured into adopting a multi-party system.[[29]](#cite_note-32) However, the BSPP government was overthrown in a [military coup d'état](/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "Coup d'état") on 18 September 1988. The military then established the [State Law and Order Restoration Council](/wiki/State_Peace_and_Development_Council "State Peace and Development Council") (SLORC) and violently cracked down on protesters, ending all demonstrations by 21 September 1988.[[30]](#cite_note-33)

Authorities in Myanmar claimed that around 350 people were killed,[[31]](#cite_note-34)[[32]](#cite_note-35) while opposition groups claimed thousands died in the protests at the hands of the military.[[33]](#cite_note-Ferrara-36)[[34]](#cite_note-BBC32013-37)[[35]](#cite_note-Wintle-38) According to *[The Economist](/wiki/The_Economist "The Economist")*, over 3,000 people were killed in the demonstrations.[[36]](#cite_note-economist-39) Despite its violent suppression of the 8888 Uprising, the new military junta agreed to ceasefire agreements with certain insurgent groups after the demonstrations ceased.

[Aung San Suu Kyi](/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi") emerged from the 8888 Uprising as a symbol of Myanmar's pro-democracy movement, leading the country's largest opposition party, the [National League for Democracy](/wiki/National_League_for_Democracy "National League for Democracy") (NLD). The military junta arranged a [general election in 1990](/wiki/1990_Myanmar_general_election "1990 Myanmar general election") to elect members of a parliament-sized [constitutional committee](/wiki/Constituent_assembly "Constituent assembly") which would draft a new constitution. The NLD won a supermajority of the seats, but the military junta, surprised by the outcome, refused to recognise the results and placed Aung San Suu Kyi under [house arrest](/wiki/House_arrest "House arrest").[[37]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAung-ThwinAung-Thwin2012266–267-40)[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteinberg201090–93-41)

### SLORC / SPDC junta (1988–2011)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Burma1989.png/250px-Burma1989.png)](/wiki/File:Burma1989.png)

Map of insurgent activity in Burma in 1989

After voiding the results of the 1990 election, the military junta consolidated its rule over Myanmar. The SLORC was abolished in 1997 and replaced with the [State Peace and Development Council](/wiki/State_Peace_and_Development_Council "State Peace and Development Council") (SPDC), which consisted of eleven senior military officers.[[39]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteinberg2001-42)[*[page needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")*]

In the 1990s, the Tatmadaw severely weakened ethnic insurgent groups, destroying most of their bases and strongholds.[[40]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFlintKirsch201199-43)

In 2006, the Tatmadaw launched a large-scale military offensive against the KNU's armed wing, the [Karen National Liberation Army](/wiki/Karen_National_Liberation_Army "Karen National Liberation Army") (KNLA). The clashes resulted in the displacement of hundreds of thousands of civilians in Kayin State. According to one estimate, approximately half a million people were displaced due to fighting between government forces and the KNU, and the forcible relocation of villages by the government.[[41]](#cite_note-blogs.cfr.org-44)[[42]](#cite_note-Ray_Pagnucco_and_Jennifer_Peters-45)

In 2007, hundreds of thousands of monks protested against the military junta's rule, and called for [free elections](/wiki/Free_election "Free election"), [minority rights](/wiki/Minority_rights "Minority rights") and the release of [political prisoners](/wiki/Political_prisoner "Political prisoner") in an event now known as the [Saffron Revolution](/wiki/Saffron_Revolution "Saffron Revolution").[[43]](#cite_note-August,_2007_Burma-46) The protest originally began in response to the government's removal of price subsidies for [compressed natural gas](/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas "Compressed natural gas").[[44]](#cite_note-Fuel-47)

The Tatmadaw attacked [Kokang](/wiki/Kokang "Kokang") in 2009, causing the [Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army](/wiki/Myanmar_National_Democratic_Alliance_Army "Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army") to lose control of the area and forcing 30,000 [refugees](/wiki/Refugee "Refugee") to flee to neighbouring [Yunnan](/wiki/Yunnan "Yunnan"), China.[[45]](#cite_note-CNN-2t-48)

In 2010, Tatmadaw forces [clashed violently](/wiki/2010%E2%80%932012_Myanmar_border_clashes "2010–2012 Myanmar border clashes") with [DKBA-5](/wiki/DKBA-5 "DKBA-5"), resulting in nearly 10,000 refugees fleeing to Thailand to escape the violent conflict.

### Civilian government (2011–2021)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/SAZs_%26_SAD_of_Burma.png/250px-SAZs_%26_SAD_of_Burma.png)](/wiki/File:SAZs_%26_SAD_of_Burma.png)

The [five self-administered zones](/wiki/Self-administered_zone "Self-administered zone") and [one self-administered division](/wiki/Wa_Self-Administered_Division "Wa Self-Administered Division") created by the 2008 constitution.

The government introduced a new [constitution](/wiki/Constitution_of_Myanmar "Constitution of Myanmar") in 2008 and instigated a [period of political reforms from 2011 to 2015](/wiki/2011%E2%80%932015_Myanmar_political_reforms "2011–2015 Myanmar political reforms"), with thousands of [political prisoners](/wiki/Political_prisoner "Political prisoner") being released, including Aung San Suu Kyi. The 2008 constitution created [five self-administered zones](/wiki/Self-administered_zone "Self-administered zone") and [one self-administered division](/wiki/Wa_Self-Administered_Division "Wa Self-Administered Division") for six ethnic minority groups.[[46]](#cite_note-49) In November 2014, the NLD attempted to make amendments to the constitution, in response to a clause that made Aung San Suu Kyi ineligible to become [President of Myanmar](/wiki/President_of_Myanmar "President of Myanmar") if her party won an election. These amendments however, were rejected.[[47]](#cite_note-50)

In 2013, large [anti-Muslim riots](/wiki/2013_Myanmar_anti-Muslim_riots "2013 Myanmar anti-Muslim riots") flared up in various cities across Myanmar. The violence coincided with the rise of the Buddhist nationalist [969 Movement](/wiki/969_Movement "969 Movement"), led by Sayadaw U [Wirathu](/wiki/Wirathu "Wirathu").[[48]](#cite_note-51)

In early 2015, the Burmese government invited 15 insurgent groups to negotiate a "[Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement](/wiki/Nationwide_Ceasefire_Agreement "Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement")". The draft was agreed upon by a majority of the invited parties on 31 March 2015,[[49]](#cite_note-52) and the agreement was signed by Burmese president [Thein Sein](/wiki/Thein_Sein "Thein Sein") and the leaders of eight insurgent groups on 15 October 2015.[[50]](#cite_note-53)

In Shan State, the military continued to engage the MNDAA during the [2015 Kokang offensive](/wiki/2015_Kokang_offensive "2015 Kokang offensive").

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Aung_Sang_Suu_Kyi_at_21st_Century_Panglong_%28VOA%29.jpg/250px-Aung_Sang_Suu_Kyi_at_21st_Century_Panglong_%28VOA%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:Aung_Sang_Suu_Kyi_at_21st_Century_Panglong_(VOA).jpg)

[State Counsellor](/wiki/State_Counsellor_of_Myanmar "State Counsellor of Myanmar") [Aung San Suu Kyi](/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi") makes her opening speech at the second session of the [Union Peace Conference – 21st Century Panglong](/wiki/Union_Peace_Conference_%E2%80%93_21st_Century_Panglong "Union Peace Conference – 21st Century Panglong"), 24 May 2017.

After the end of political reforms in 2015, the government began hosting a number of peace conferences, including the [Union Peace Conference – 21st Century Panglong](/wiki/Union_Peace_Conference_%E2%80%93_21st_Century_Panglong "Union Peace Conference – 21st Century Panglong"). The first session of the conference was held in 2016, with three follow-up sessions held in 2017, 2018, and 2020.[[51]](#cite_note-Xinhua-2020-54) The conference was criticised by opposition groups, including some attendees, for not addressing the main proposals made by ceasefire groups, and for excluding the country's largest insurgent groups which remained actively hostile.[[52]](#cite_note-55)[[53]](#cite_note-56) Critics further argued that Myanmar's constitution granted too much power to the military, and was preventing the country from achieving peace and meaningful democratic reforms.[[54]](#cite_note-57)[[55]](#cite_note-58)

On 9 October 2016, the [Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army](/wiki/Arakan_Rohingya_Salvation_Army "Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army") (ARSA) [launched its first attack](/wiki/Northern_Rakhine_State_clashes#2016 "Northern Rakhine State clashes") on Burmese border posts along the [Bangladesh–Myanmar border](/wiki/Bangladesh%E2%80%93Myanmar_border "Bangladesh–Myanmar border"), killing nine border officers.[[56]](#cite_note-BBC9102016-59) This prompted the Tatmadaw to begin massive "clearance operations" in northern Rakhine State, which intensified following [a second large-scale attack](/wiki/Northern_Rakhine_State_clashes#25_August_2017_attacks "Northern Rakhine State clashes") by ARSA on 25 August 2017.[[57]](#cite_note-BBC4-60)[[58]](#cite_note-AP1-61)[[59]](#cite_note-Reuters6-62) The subsequent violence has sparked [international outcry](/wiki/International_reactions_to_the_Rohingya_genocide "International reactions to the Rohingya genocide") and was described as an [ethnic cleansing](/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing "Ethnic cleansing") by the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights.[[60]](#cite_note-aung_san_skip_20170913_npr_org-63)[[61]](#cite_note-64)

In late November 2016, the [Northern Alliance](/wiki/Northern_Alliance_(Myanmar) "Northern Alliance (Myanmar)")—which consists of four [insurgent groups](/wiki/Insurgency "Insurgency"), the [Arakan Army](/wiki/Arakan_Army_(Kachin_State) "Arakan Army (Kachin State)") (AA), the [Kachin Independence Army](/wiki/Kachin_Independence_Army "Kachin Independence Army") (KIA), the [Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army](/wiki/Myanmar_National_Democratic_Alliance_Army "Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army") (MNDAA) and the [Ta'ang National Liberation Army](/wiki/Ta%27ang_National_Liberation_Army "Ta'ang National Liberation Army") (TNLA)—[attacked towns and border posts](/wiki/Muse_offensive "Muse offensive") along the [China–Myanmar border](/wiki/China%E2%80%93Myanmar_border "China–Myanmar border") in [Muse Township](/wiki/Mu_Se_Township "Mu Se Township"), northern Shan State.[[62]](#cite_note-Nadi-65)[[63]](#cite_note-66) The insurgents captured the town of [Mong Ko](/wiki/Mong_Ko "Mong Ko") on 25 November 2016[[64]](#cite_note-67) and maintained control of it until they withdrew from the town on 4 December 2016 to avoid civilian casualties from [airstrikes](/wiki/Airstrike "Airstrike") by the [Myanmar Air Force](/wiki/Myanmar_Air_Force "Myanmar Air Force").[[65]](#cite_note-68)[[66]](#cite_note-69)

On 15 August 2019, Northern Alliance insurgents attacked a military college in [Nawnghkio Township](/wiki/Nawnghkio_Township "Nawnghkio Township"), killing 15.[[67]](#cite_note-70)[[68]](#cite_note-71)[[69]](#cite_note-72)[[70]](#cite_note-73) Further clashes occurred in the following days,[[71]](#cite_note-74)[[72]](#cite_note-75)[[73]](#cite_note-76)[[74]](#cite_note-77) with Myanmar's military warning there could be a "full-scale war" in Shan State if the Northern Alliance did not halt their attacks.[[75]](#cite_note-78)

### SAC junta (2021–present)

Main articles: [Myanmar civil war (2021–present)](/wiki/Myanmar_civil_war_(2021%E2%80%93present) "Myanmar civil war (2021–present)") and [Myanmar protests (2021–present)](/wiki/Myanmar_protests_(2021%E2%80%93present) "Myanmar protests (2021–present)")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi_%26_Min_Aung_Hlaing_collage.jpg/250px-Aung_San_Suu_Kyi_%26_Min_Aung_Hlaing_collage.jpg)](/wiki/File:Aung_San_Suu_Kyi_%26_Min_Aung_Hlaing_collage.jpg)

[State Counsellor](/wiki/State_Counsellor_of_Myanmar "State Counsellor of Myanmar") [Aung San Suu Kyi](/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi "Aung San Suu Kyi") (left) was deposed in a military coup led by Senior General [Min Aung Hlaing](/wiki/Min_Aung_Hlaing "Min Aung Hlaing") (right) on 1 February 2021.

On the early morning of 1 February 2021, the civilian government led by the NLD was [overthrown in a military coup d'état](/wiki/2021_Myanmar_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2021 Myanmar coup d'état"), and the Tatmadaw's commander-in-chief, Senior General [Min Aung Hlaing](/wiki/Min_Aung_Hlaing "Min Aung Hlaing"), became the head of state. Aung San Suu Kyi and several other senior members of her government were arrested by the military during the coup.[[76]](#cite_note-NPR-79) [Mass protests](/wiki/2021_Myanmar_protests "2021 Myanmar protests") followed, with demonstrators demanding the resignation of Min Aung Hlaing and the newly created [State Administration Council](/wiki/State_Administration_Council "State Administration Council") (SAC), the release of those arrested in the coup, and the restoration of the civilian government.[[77]](#cite_note-The_Diplomat-80)

Anti-coup protesters have armed themselves with slingshots, molotov cocktails, and makeshift shields.[[78]](#cite_note-MMN30321-81) In late March 2021, it was reported that dozens of protesters had travelled to Myanmar's border areas to train under one of the country's many insurgent groups, elevating the risk of a countrywide civil war.[[79]](#cite_note-82) The civilian government-in-exile, the [Committee Representing the Pyidaungsu Hluttaw](/wiki/Committee_Representing_Pyidaungsu_Hluttaw "Committee Representing Pyidaungsu Hluttaw") (CRPH), has proposed the formation of a "federal armed force" to combat the military.[[78]](#cite_note-MMN30321-81)

One of the first instances of armed resistance by protesters occurred in and around the town of [Kalay](/wiki/Kalay "Kalay"), [Sagaing Region](/wiki/Sagaing_Region "Sagaing Region"). After the Tatmadaw raided a [protest camp](/wiki/Protest_camp "Protest camp") in Kalay on 28 March 2021, protesters [fought back](/wiki/2021_Kalay_clashes "2021 Kalay clashes") with hunting rifles and homemade firearms.[[80]](#cite_note-83) Several insurgent groups, notably the Kachin Independence Army and the Karen National Liberation Army, have also resumed or escalated their attacks against the Tatmadaw in response to the coup.[[81]](#cite_note-84)[[82]](#cite_note-Reuters270421-85)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Military_situation_in_Myanmar%2C_December_2023.png/250px-Military_situation_in_Myanmar%2C_December_2023.png)](/wiki/File:Military_situation_in_Myanmar,_December_2023.png)

Map of insurgent activity in Myanmar in December 2023

Seven signatories of the Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement announced on 4 April 2021 that they had decided to join the [National Unity Government](/wiki/National_Unity_Government_of_Myanmar "National Unity Government of Myanmar"), including the [All Burma Student Democratic Front](/wiki/All_Burma_Student_Democratic_Front "All Burma Student Democratic Front") and the Karen National Union.[[83]](#cite_note-86) Some of the groups have resumed hostilities towards the junta government.

The [Chinland Defence Force](/wiki/Chinland_Defence_Force "Chinland Defence Force") began its armed resistance in [Mindat](/wiki/Mindat,_Chin_State "Mindat, Chin State") and [Hakha](/wiki/Hakha "Hakha"), Chin State, on 26 April 2021.[[84]](#cite_note-87)

On 5 May 2021, the National Unity Government declared the formation of an armed wing, the [People's Defence Force (PDF)](/wiki/People%27s_Defence_Force_(Myanmar) "People's Defence Force (Myanmar)"), to protect its supporters from military junta attacks and as a first step towards a Federal Union Army.[[85]](#cite_note-88) It clashed with the Tatmadaw in the town of [Muse](/wiki/Muse,_Myanmar "Muse, Myanmar") on 23 May, killing at least 13 members of Myanmar's security forces.[[86]](#cite_note-89)[[87]](#cite_note-90) Members of the Karenni People's Defence Force (KPDF) in [Kayah State](/wiki/Kayah_State "Kayah State") also clashed with the Tatmadaw near the state capital of [Loikaw](/wiki/Loikaw "Loikaw").[[88]](#cite_note-91)[[89]](#cite_note-92)

The Communist Party of Burma rearmed itself and announced the creation of its new armed wing, the People's Liberation Army, in late 2021.[[90]](#cite_note-93)[[91]](#cite_note-94)

[ACLED](/wiki/ACLED "ACLED") estimated that 11,000 people had been killed from February to December 2021.[[92]](#cite_note-95)

### Chin State and Sagaing Region

See also: [Zomi nationalism](/wiki/Zomi_nationalism "Zomi nationalism") and [Chin theater](/wiki/Chin_theater "Chin theater")

The [Mizo](/wiki/Mizo_people "Mizo people") people of [Mizoram](/wiki/Mizoram "Mizoram"), India, the [Chin people](/wiki/Chin_people "Chin people") of Myanmar, and the [Kuki people](/wiki/Kuki_people "Kuki people") are all [Zo people](/wiki/Zo_people "Zo people") who share a common culture and history.

In 1960, the Chin Liberation Army was founded by Tun Kho Pum Baite to unify the Chin-inhabited areas, while the [Mizo National Front](/wiki/Mizo_National_Front "Mizo National Front") (MNF) fought for Mizo independence. The [Chin National Army](/wiki/Chin_National_Army "Chin National Army") (CNF) was formed in 1988. It signed a ceasefire agreement with the Chin State government in 2012. The [Kuki National Army](/wiki/Kuki_National_Army "Kuki National Army") (KNA) was also founded in 1988 with the goal of creating Kuki autonomy in Myanmar and India.

Several separatist groups [fighting the Indian government](/wiki/Insurgency_in_Northeast_India "Insurgency in Northeast India") in Northeast India also operate from bases in Myanmar, such as the [Zomi Revolutionary Army](/wiki/Zomi_Revolutionary_Army "Zomi Revolutionary Army"), the [United Liberation Front of Assam](/wiki/United_Liberation_Front_of_Assam "United Liberation Front of Assam") (ULFA), and the [National Socialist Council of Nagaland](/wiki/National_Socialist_Council_of_Nagaland "National Socialist Council of Nagaland") (NSCN). These groups frequently cross into India via the porous border.[[93]](#cite_note-96)

In June 2019, Tatmadaw troops, in coordination with the Indian Army, carried out operations against the NSCN headquarters in [Taga](/w/index.php?title=Taga,_Myanmar&action=edit&redlink=1 "Taga, Myanmar (page does not exist)"), in the [Naga Self-Administered Zone](/wiki/Naga_Self-Administered_Zone "Naga Self-Administered Zone") of Sagaing Region.[[94]](#cite_note-EconIn16619-97)

### Kachin State

Main article: [Kachin conflict](/wiki/Kachin_conflict "Kachin conflict")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Kachin_Independence_Army_cadets_in_Laiza_%28Paul_Vrieze-VOA%29.jpg/250px-Kachin_Independence_Army_cadets_in_Laiza_%28Paul_Vrieze-VOA%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:Kachin_Independence_Army_cadets_in_Laiza_(Paul_Vrieze-VOA).jpg)

Cadets of the Kachin Independence Army (KIA) preparing for military drills at the group's headquarters in [Laiza](/wiki/Laiza "Laiza")

The [Kachin people](/wiki/Kachin_people "Kachin people") are a major ethnic minority in Myanmar who primarily inhabit the mountainous northern regions of the [Kachin Hills](/wiki/Kachin_Hills "Kachin Hills") in [Kachin State](/wiki/Kachin_State "Kachin State"). Kachin regular soldiers previously formed a significant part of the Myanmar Army; however, after Ne Win's regime seized power in 1962, many Kachin soldiers defected from the military and reorganised with already active Kachin insurgents to form the Kachin Independence Army (KIA), under the [Kachin Independence Organisation](/wiki/Kachin_Independence_Organisation "Kachin Independence Organisation") (KIO). Religious tensions have also been a source of conflict, as Kachin people have historically been predominantly Christian, while the majority [Bamar people](/wiki/Bamar_people "Bamar people") have been predominantly [Buddhist](/wiki/Buddhist "Buddhist").[[95]](#cite_note-98)

Ceasefire agreements have been signed between the KIA and the government several times; most notably a ceasefire signed in 1994, that lasted for 17 years until June 2011, when government forces attacked KIA positions along the [Taping River](/wiki/Taping_River "Taping River"), east of [Bhamo](/wiki/Bhamo "Bhamo"), Kachin State.[[96]](#cite_note-hrwarchive1-99) As a result of the ceasefire breakdown, Kachin State has faced waves of internal displacement, with over 90,000 internally displaced people spread across over 150 camps or camp-like settings as of April 2017. Many IDP camps are located in non-government controlled areas with severely restricted access.[[97]](#cite_note-100) The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre (IDMC) estimates that in April and May 2018, over 14,000 people were displaced from fighting between the KIA and the Tatmadaw.[[98]](#cite_note-:0-101)

Clashes between the KIA and the Tatmadaw in 2012 resulted in around 2,500 casualties (both civilian and military), 211 of whom were government soldiers. The violence resulted in the displacement of nearly 100,000 civilians and the complete or partial abandonment of 364 villages.[[99]](#cite_note-MMoverview-102)[[100]](#cite_note-kachinnews2-103)[[101]](#cite_note-dailytimes2012-104)[[102]](#cite_note-105)[[103]](#cite_note-kachinwomen1-106)

Government forces attacked the Kachin Independence Army's headquarters near the city of [Laiza](/wiki/Laiza "Laiza") on 19 November 2014, killing at least 22 KIA insurgents, according to the government.[[104]](#cite_note-107)

[N'Ban La](/wiki/N%27Ban_La "N'Ban La") became the KIO's chairman and the KIA's second-in-command in January 2018. [Htang Gam Shawng](/wiki/Htang_Gam_Shawng "Htang Gam Shawng") retained his position as the KIA's commander-in-chief.[[105]](#cite_note-Kumbun-108)

### Kayah State

In 1957, pro-independence groups in Karenni State (present-day [Kayah State](/wiki/Kayah_State "Kayah State")) founded the [Karenni National Progressive Party](/wiki/Karenni_National_Progressive_Party "Karenni National Progressive Party") (KNPP). An accompanying armed wing, the [Karenni Army](/wiki/Karenni_Army "Karenni Army"), was established shortly after to fight for the self-determination of the [Karenni people](/wiki/Karenni_people "Karenni people").[[106]](#cite_note-KAJane-109) The Karenni Army and Tatmadaw have fought in the region ever since, apart from a brief three-month ceasefire in 1995.[[107]](#cite_note-uca-110) Rivals to the KNPP include the leftist [Kayan New Land Party](/wiki/Kayan_New_Land_Party "Kayan New Land Party") (KNLP) and [Karenni National People's Liberation Front](/wiki/Karenni_National_People%27s_Liberation_Front "Karenni National People's Liberation Front") (KNPLF), both of whom signed ceasefires with the government in the 1990s.[[108]](#cite_note-Irrawaddy_ceasefires-111)[[109]](#cite_note-Mizzima_KNPLF-112)

The Karenni Army is currently led by General Bee Htoo,[[106]](#cite_note-KAJane-109) and consists of roughly between 500[[110]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTERotberg1998-113)[*[page needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")*] and 1,500 troops.[[111]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKeenan20121-114)

The conflict escalated following the 2021 military coup, with the civilian-led Karenni People's Defence Force (KPDF) opening up a new front in northern Kayah State. After Tatmadaw troops [attacked and burned down](/wiki/Scorched_earth "Scorched earth") several villages in the region, KPDF fighters launched an offensive against the Tatmadaw, seizing and destroying several military outposts.[[112]](#cite_note-115)

### Kayin State

Main article: [Karen conflict](/wiki/Karen_conflict "Karen conflict")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/KNLA_medic_treating_displaced_civilians_%28Steve_Sandford-VOA%29.jpg/250px-KNLA_medic_treating_displaced_civilians_%28Steve_Sandford-VOA%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:KNLA_medic_treating_displaced_civilians_(Steve_Sandford-VOA).jpg)

A KNLA medic treats [IDPs](/wiki/Internally_displaced_person "Internally displaced person") in [Hpapun District](/wiki/Hpapun_District "Hpapun District"), [Kayin State](/wiki/Kayin_State "Kayin State").

The [Karen people](/wiki/Karen_people "Karen people") of [Kayin State](/wiki/Kayin_State "Kayin State") (formerly Karen State) in eastern Myanmar are the third largest ethnic group in Myanmar, consisting of roughly 7% of the country's total population. Karen insurgent groups have fought for independence and self-determination since 1949. In 1949, the [commander-in-chief](/wiki/Commander-in-chief "Commander-in-chief") of the Tatmadaw General [Smith Dun](/wiki/Smith_Dun "Smith Dun"), an ethnic Karen, was fired because of the rise of Karen opposition groups, which furthered ethnic tensions. He was replaced by Ne Win, a Bamar [nationalist](/wiki/Nationalist "Nationalist") who would later rule Myanmar as a military dictator.[[113]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1991118-116)

The government of Myanmar has been accused of using "[scorched earth](/wiki/Scorched_earth "Scorched earth")" tactics against Karen civilians in the past, including (but not limited to) burning down entire villages, planting [land mines](/wiki/Land_mine "Land mine"), using civilians as slave labour, using civilians as minesweepers and the rape and murder of Karen women.[[114]](#cite_note-NYFree-117) According to a report by legal firm [DLA Piper](/wiki/DLA_Piper "DLA Piper"), whose report was presented to the [United Nations Security Council](/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council"), these tactics against the Karen can be identified as [ethnic cleansing](/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing "Ethnic cleansing"). The government had however, denied these claims.[[115]](#cite_note-DLApiper-118)

The initial aim of the [Karen National Union](/wiki/Karen_National_Union "Karen National Union") (KNU) and its armed wing the [Karen National Liberation Army](/wiki/Karen_National_Liberation_Army "Karen National Liberation Army") (KNLA) was to create independent state for the Karen people. However, since 1976 they have instead called for a [federal union](/wiki/Federation "Federation") with fair Karen representation and the self-determination of the Karen people.[[21]](#cite_note-KNUabout-24)

In 1995, the main headquarters and operating bases of the KNU were mostly destroyed or captured by the government, forcing the KNLA to operate from the jungles of Kayin State. Until 1995, the [Thai government](/wiki/Government_of_Thailand "Government of Thailand") supported insurgents across the [Myanmar–Thailand border](/wiki/Myanmar%E2%80%93Thailand_border "Myanmar–Thailand border"), but soon stopped its support due to a new major economic deal with Myanmar.[[116]](#cite_note-Lintner_Thai-119)

The KNU signed the Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement with the government of Myanmar on 15 October 2015, along with seven other insurgent groups.[[117]](#cite_note-rfaNCA-120) However, in March 2018, the government of Myanmar violated the agreement by sending 400 Tatmadaw soldiers into KNU-held territory to build a road connecting two military bases.[[118]](#cite_note-121) Armed clashes erupted between the KNU and the [Myanmar Army](/wiki/Myanmar_Army "Myanmar Army") in the Ler Mu Plaw area of [Hpapun District](/wiki/Hpapun_District "Hpapun District"), resulting in the displacement of 2,000 people.[[119]](#cite_note-122) On 17 May 2018, the Tatmadaw agreed to "temporarily postpone" their road project and to withdraw troops from the area.[[120]](#cite_note-123)

The KNU resumed its fight against the Myanmar government following the 2021 military coup. On 27 April 2021, KNU insurgents [captured a military base](/wiki/April_2021_Tatmadaw%E2%80%93KNLA_clashes "April 2021 Tatmadaw–KNLA clashes") on the west bank of the [Salween River](/wiki/Salween_River "Salween River"), which forms Myanmar's border with Thailand. The Tatmadaw later retaliated with airstrikes on KNU positions. There were no casualties reported by either side.[[82]](#cite_note-Reuters270421-85)

### Mon State

The [Mon people](/wiki/Mon_people "Mon people") have sought self-determination since Myanmar gained independence in 1948, initially under the Mon People's Front and from 1962 through the [New Mon State Party](/wiki/New_Mon_State_Party "New Mon State Party") (NMSP). The [Mon National Liberation Army](/wiki/Mon_National_Liberation_Army "Mon National Liberation Army") (MNLA) has been fighting government forces since 1949. It signed the Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement in 2015 and has had minor skirmishes with the Karen National Liberation Army (KNLA).

### Rakhine State

See also: [Conflict in Rakhine State (2016–present)](/wiki/Conflict_in_Rakhine_State_(2016%E2%80%93present) "Conflict in Rakhine State (2016–present)")

#### Rakhine insurgency

Insurgent groups of the [Rakhine](/wiki/Rakhine_people "Rakhine people") (or Arakanese),[[121]](#cite_note-MMalp-124) [Chin](/wiki/Chin_people "Chin people"),[[122]](#cite_note-MMcnf-125) and [Rohingya](/wiki/Rohingya_people "Rohingya people")[[123]](#cite_note-RBangladesh-126) ethnic minorities have fought against the government for self-determination in [Rakhine State](/wiki/Rakhine_State "Rakhine State") since the early 1950s. The region was a stronghold of the [Red Flag Communist Party](/wiki/Communist_Party_(Burma) "Communist Party (Burma)") until its defeat by the Tatmadaw in 1978. Its successor, the [Communist Party of Arakan](/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Arakan "Communist Party of Arakan") continued to wage an insurgency in Rakhine State until the 1990s.[[124]](#cite_note-Thadin-127)

Ethnic Rakhine insurgent groups, such as the [Arakan Army](/wiki/Arakan_Army_(Kachin_State) "Arakan Army (Kachin State)") and [Arakan Liberation Army](/wiki/Arakan_Liberation_Army "Arakan Liberation Army") (ALA), continue to fight against the government, though major violence has been rare since political reforms and peace talks.[*[when?](/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")*] The Arakan Army, founded in 2009, is currently the largest insurgent group in Rakhine State, with around 7,000 fighters.[[125]](#cite_note-voaAA-128)

On 4 January 2019, around 300 Arakan Army insurgents launched pre-dawn attacks on four border police outposts—Kyaung Taung, Nga Myin Taw, Ka Htee La and Kone Myint—in northern [Buthidaung Township](/wiki/Buthidaung_Township "Buthidaung Township").[[126]](#cite_note-129) Thirteen members of the [Border Guard Police](/wiki/Border_Guard_Police "Border Guard Police") (BGP) were killed and nine others were injured,[[127]](#cite_note-130)[[128]](#cite_note-131)[[129]](#cite_note-132) while 40 firearms and more than 10,000 rounds of ammunition were looted. The Arakan Army later stated that it had captured nine BGP personnel and five civilians, and that three of its fighters were also killed in the attacks.[[130]](#cite_note-WSJ4119-133)[[131]](#cite_note-134)

Following the attacks, the Office of the President of Myanmar held a high-level meeting on national security in the capital [Naypyidaw](/wiki/Naypyidaw "Naypyidaw") on 7 January 2019, and instructed the Defence Ministry to increase troop deployments in the areas that were attacked and to use aircraft if necessary.[[132]](#cite_note-135) Subsequent clashes between the [Myanmar Army](/wiki/Myanmar_Army "Myanmar Army") and the Arakan Army were reported in [Maungdaw](/wiki/Maungdaw_Township "Maungdaw Township"), [Buthidaung](/wiki/Buthidaung_Township "Buthidaung Township"), [Kyauktaw](/wiki/Kyauktaw_Township "Kyauktaw Township"), [Rathedaung](/wiki/Rathedaung_Township "Rathedaung Township") and [Ponnagyun Townships](/wiki/Ponnagyun_Township "Ponnagyun Township"), forcing out over 5,000 civilians from their homes,[[133]](#cite_note-136)[[134]](#cite_note-137) hundreds of whom (mostly Rakhine and [Khami](/wiki/Mro_people_(Awa_Khami) "Mro people (Awa Khami)")) have fled across the border into Bangladesh.[[135]](#cite_note-138) Civilian casualties,[[136]](#cite_note-139)[[137]](#cite_note-140) arbitrary beatings[[138]](#cite_note-141) and detentions of ethnic Rakhines,[[139]](#cite_note-142) forced seizures of property,[[140]](#cite_note-143) and blockage of food aid and medical relief by the Tatmadaw have also been reported.[[141]](#cite_note-144)

#### Rohingya insurgency

Main articles: [Rohingya conflict](/wiki/Rohingya_conflict "Rohingya conflict") and [Rohingya genocide](/wiki/Rohingya_genocide "Rohingya genocide")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Rohingya_mujahid_surrenders_%281961%29.jpg/250px-Rohingya_mujahid_surrenders_%281961%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:Rohingya_mujahid_surrenders_(1961).jpg)

A Rohingya mujahid surrenders his weapon to Brigadier-General [Aung Gyi](/wiki/Aung_Gyi "Aung Gyi"), 4 July 1961.

Rohingya insurgents have been fighting against local government forces and other insurgent groups in northern [Rakhine State](/wiki/Rakhine_State "Rakhine State") since 1948, with ongoing [religious violence](/wiki/Religious_violence "Religious violence") between the predominantly [Muslim](/wiki/Islam_in_Myanmar "Islam in Myanmar") Rohingyas and the [Buddhist](/wiki/Buddhism_in_Myanmar "Buddhism in Myanmar") Rakhines fuelling the conflict.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*]

Following the independence of Myanmar, Rohingya [mujahideen](/wiki/Mujahideen "Mujahideen") fought against government forces in an attempt to secede and annex Rohingya-inhabited regions to [East Pakistan](/wiki/East_Pakistan "East Pakistan"). Between 1949 and 1954, the Burmese military launched several operations in order to regain control of the region. By the end of the 1950s, the mujahideen had lost most of their momentum and support, and most of their fighters had surrendered by 1961.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*]

Ne Win's military junta turned increasingly hostile towards the Rohingyas. The authorities launched large-scale military operations in order to expel insurgents and so-called "foreigners" from Arakan, such as [Operation Dragon King](/wiki/Operation_Dragon_King "Operation Dragon King") in 1978 and [Operation Pyi Thaya](/wiki/Operation_Pyi_Thaya "Operation Pyi Thaya") in 1991[[142]](#cite_note-econ102012-145)

The legal and political rights of the Rohingya people have been an underlying issue during the conflict, with spontaneous bouts of violence, such as the [2012 Rakhine State riots](/wiki/2012_Rakhine_State_riots "2012 Rakhine State riots") and the [2013 Myanmar anti-Muslim riots](/wiki/2013_Myanmar_anti-Muslim_riots "2013 Myanmar anti-Muslim riots"), periodically occurring as a result. Despite making up the majority of the population in the three northern townships of Rakhine State,[[123]](#cite_note-RBangladesh-126) the Rohingyas are frequently the targets of religiously motivated attacks. The [1982 Nationality law](/wiki/Nationality_law_of_Myanmar "Nationality law of Myanmar") did not recognise the Rohingyas as an ethnic group which is native to Myanmar.[[142]](#cite_note-econ102012-145) As a result, the Rohingyas cannot apply for Burmese citizenship and few laws exist to protect their rights.[[143]](#cite_note-146)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Myanmar_police_patrolling_in_Maungdaw.jpg/250px-Myanmar_police_patrolling_in_Maungdaw.jpg)](/wiki/File:Myanmar_police_patrolling_in_Maungdaw.jpg)

Members of the [Myanmar Police Force](/wiki/Myanmar_Police_Force "Myanmar Police Force") patrolling in Maungdaw in September 2017.

On 9 October 2016, unidentified insurgents attacked three Burmese border posts along Myanmar's border with Bangladesh, starting a [new armed conflict](/wiki/Northern_Rakhine_State_clashes "Northern Rakhine State clashes") in northern Rakhine State. According to government officials in the border town of [Maungdaw](/wiki/Maungdaw "Maungdaw"), the attackers looted several dozen firearms and ammunition from the border posts, and they also brandished knives and homemade slingshots that fired metal bolts. The attacks left nine border officers and "several insurgents" dead.[[56]](#cite_note-BBC9102016-59) On 11 October 2016, four Tatmadaw soldiers were killed on the third day of fighting.[[144]](#cite_note-BBC12102016-147) A newly emerging insurgent group, the [Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army](/wiki/Arakan_Rohingya_Salvation_Army "Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army") (ARSA), claimed responsibility for the attacks a week later.[[145]](#cite_note-BKP1-148)

During the early hours of 25 August 2017, ARSA insurgents launched coordinated attacks on 24 police posts and the 552nd Light Infantry Battalion army base, killing a dozen people.[[57]](#cite_note-BBC4-60)[[58]](#cite_note-AP1-61)[[59]](#cite_note-Reuters6-62) In response, the Tatmadaw launched "clearance operations" in northern Rakhine State, which critics argued targeted Rohingya civilians rather than insurgents.[[146]](#cite_note-myanmar_accused_2017_09_10_ap_chicago_tribune-149)[[147]](#cite_note-hundreds_dead_2017_09_10_washpost_chg_trib-150)[[148]](#cite_note-TroopsOpenFire-151) Following the violence, 200,000 civilians remained trapped in the region without adequate access to markets, livelihoods, services and medical care.[[149]](#cite_note-152)[[150]](#cite_note-People_in_Need-153)

### Shan State

The [Shan people](/wiki/Shan_people "Shan people") are the largest ethnic group in [Shan State](/wiki/Shan_State "Shan State") and the second largest in Myanmar. They were one of several ethnic groups consulted by Aung San during negotiations leading up to the Panglong Agreement, which gave the Shan leaders the option to split from Myanmar a decade after independence if they were unsatisfied with the central government.[[17]](#cite_note-Maung-20) This was, however, not honoured by the post-independence government following Aung San's assassination.[[16]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMyint2006274–289-19)

Frustrated by a decade of fruitless negotiations, a group of 39 Shan led by Saw Yanda[[151]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYawnghwe2010[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidemjivsbStD0CpgPA213_213]-154) (a Shan from Yunnan, China; also known as Chao Noi, Sao Noi, and Saw Yanna) formed an armed resistance movement on 21 May 1958.[[152]](#cite_note-Black_Fields-155) It became known as the *Noom Suk Harn* (or *Noom Seik Harn*[[153]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELintner1984413-156)), meaning "brave young warriors".[[152]](#cite_note-Black_Fields-155) In 1960, the movement suffered an internal split between the "rough and uneducated" Saw Yanda and a group of young intellectuals, and the latter subsequently formed the Shan State Independence Army (SSIA).[[154]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELintner1984414-157) Aside from the remnants of the *Noom Suk Harn* and the SSIA, there was also the Shan National United Front (SNUF), which operated primarily in southern Shan State. The SSIA, the SNUF, and the Kokang Force (a local army consisting of [Kokang Chinese](/wiki/Kokang_Chinese "Kokang Chinese")) agreed to merge into the [Shan State Army](/wiki/Shan_State_Army "Shan State Army") (SSA) in 1964. [Sao Nang Hearn Kham](/wiki/Sao_Nang_Hearn_Kham "Sao Nang Hearn Kham"), the *Mahadevi* (queen consort) of Yawnghwe, was elected chairperson of its political wing, the Shan State War Council (SSWC).[[155]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELintner1984416-158) The SSWC was replaced by the [Shan State Progress Party](/wiki/Shan_State_Progress_Party "Shan State Progress Party") (SSPP) on 16 August 1971.[[156]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELintner1984430-159)

In northern Shan State, the SSA came into conflict with the [Communist Party of Burma](/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Burma "Communist Party of Burma") (CPB), which had long been active along Burma's border with China. During the 1960s, the Burma Army began the *Ka Kew Ye* (KKY) program, which recruited ethnic armies to combat communist insurgents, and in return, they would be permitted to engage in cross-border trade on their own terms. This led to many units of the SSA defecting to the KKY militias. The SSA also worked with opium warlords such as [Lo Hsing Han](/wiki/Lo_Hsing_Han "Lo Hsing Han") and [Khun Sa](/wiki/Khun_Sa "Khun Sa"). Internal divisions resulted in the SSA fracturing into many factions, and it collapsed in mid-1976. The SSPP later formed the pro-communist [Shan State Army – North](/wiki/Shan_State_Army_%E2%80%93_North "Shan State Army – North") (SSA-N) to continue the insurgency. After the CPB suffered an internal mutiny and collapsed in April 1989, the SSA-N signed a ceasefire agreement with the government in exchange for autonomy over the areas it controlled (officially called "Special Region 3 of Shan State"). A splinter group led by [Sai Leun](/wiki/Sai_Leun "Sai Leun") which broke away from the CPB at the same time established the [National Democratic Alliance Army](/wiki/National_Democratic_Alliance_Army "National Democratic Alliance Army") (NDAA), which controls the [Mong La area](/wiki/Mong_La_area "Mong La area") (Special Region 4) of Shan State.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Khun_Sa_%289to12%29.jpg/250px-Khun_Sa_%289to12%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:Khun_Sa_(9to12).jpg)

[Khun Sa](/wiki/Khun_Sa "Khun Sa") in 1988

In the 1960s, Khun Sa, a KKY militia leader, was permitted by the Burmese government to grow and traffic opium in return for fighting Shan and communists insurgents. He gradually became one of the most powerful insurgent leaders in Shan State. Khun Sa was captured by the Tatmadaw after plotting with the SSA in 1969, but was later released to Thailand, where he built an army near the Burmese border and became the dominant opium warlord in the Golden Triangle. After being expelled by the Thai Army in 1982, Khun Sa returned to Burma and formed the [Mong Tai Army](/wiki/Mong_Tai_Army "Mong Tai Army") (MTA) in 1985. In the 1990s, the MTA became the dominant Shan fighting force with peak strength of 20,000 soldiers. In 1991, Khun Sa declared the creation of an independent Shan State, with himself as president. However, some Shan nationalists in the MTA disagreed with his leadership and formed a rival Shan organisation, the [Shan State National Army](/wiki/Shan_State_National_Army "Shan State National Army") (SSNA).[[157]](#cite_note-160) The Mong Tai Army was quickly disbanded after the mass desertion, and Khun Sa himself surrendered to the government in 1996. He was allowed to retire in Yangon and keep his large fortune.

Refusing to surrender with Khun Sa and the MTA, Lieutenant General [Yawd Serk](/wiki/Yawd_Serk "Yawd Serk") of the [Shan United Revolutionary Army](/wiki/Shan_United_Revolutionary_Army "Shan United Revolutionary Army") (SURA) led 800 soldiers under his command to central Shan State and established the [Shan State Army – South](/wiki/Shan_State_Army_%E2%80%93_South "Shan State Army – South") (SSA-S) on 26 January 1996. He then recruited roughly a thousand more soldiers before returning to southern Shan State to establish the group's headquarters in [Loi Tai Leng](/wiki/Loi_Tai_Leng "Loi Tai Leng"). The SSA-S has since become one of the largest Shan insurgent groups in Myanmar, and has around 8,000 soldiers as of 2016.[[158]](#cite_note-MPM_stakeholders-161) Yawd Serk lead the group until his retirement on 2 February 2014. Its political wing is the [Restoration Council of Shan State](/wiki/Restoration_Council_of_Shan_State "Restoration Council of Shan State"). The SSA-S maintains a number of bases along the [Myanmar–Thailand border](/wiki/Myanmar%E2%80%93Thailand_border "Myanmar–Thailand border"), and signed a ceasefire agreement with the government on 2 December 2011.[[159]](#cite_note-MMrcss-162)

The Tatmadaw launched a military offensive named Operation Perseverance (ဇွဲမန်ဟိန်း) against insurgents in Shan State in 2011.[[160]](#cite_note-163) During the offensive, the Tatmadaw captured territory from the NDAA and the SSA-N, with the latter being involved in most of the fighting.[[161]](#cite_note-164)[[162]](#cite_note-165) While this operation was officially a response to the groups' rejections of the junta's "One Nation, One Army" policy,[[163]](#cite_note-166)[[164]](#cite_note-167)[[165]](#cite_note-168)[[166]](#cite_note-169) researchers have linked it to the military's interests in the [jade trade](/wiki/Jade_trade_in_Myanmar "Jade trade in Myanmar").[[167]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChristensenNguyenSexton2019332-170)[[168]](#cite_note-171)

In October 2015, the Tatmadaw launched a military offensive to seize territory granted to the SSA-N under a previous ceasefire. The Tatmadaw used heavy artillery and airstrikes, displacing thousands of civilians.[[169]](#cite_note-172)

Other ethnic armed groups in the region include the [Lahu Democratic Union](/wiki/Lahu_Democratic_Union "Lahu Democratic Union"), the [Ta'ang National Liberation Army](/wiki/Ta%27ang_National_Liberation_Army "Ta'ang National Liberation Army"), the [Wa National Army](/wiki/Wa_National_Army "Wa National Army") and the [Pa-O National Liberation Army](/wiki/Pa-O_National_Liberation_Army "Pa-O National Liberation Army"), of varying sizes and affiliations.

#### Kokang

Main article: [Kokang](/wiki/Kokang "Kokang")

From the 1960s to 1989, the Kokang area in northern Shan state was controlled by the Communist Party of Burma, and after the party's armed wing disbanded in 1989 it became a special region of Myanmar under the control of the [Myanmar Nationalities Democratic Alliance Army](/wiki/Myanmar_Nationalities_Democratic_Alliance_Army "Myanmar Nationalities Democratic Alliance Army") (MNDAA). The MNDAA is a [Kokang](/wiki/Kokang_people "Kokang people") insurgent group active in the [Kokang Self-Administered Zone](/wiki/Kokang_Self-Administered_Zone "Kokang Self-Administered Zone") in northern Shan State. The group signed a ceasefire agreement with the government in 1989, the same year it was founded, which lasted for two decades [until 2009](/wiki/2009_Kokang_incident "2009 Kokang incident"), when government troops entered MNDAA territory in an attempt to stop the flow of drugs through the area.[[170]](#cite_note-WP-173) Violence erupted again [in 2015](/wiki/2015_Kokang_offensive "2015 Kokang offensive"), when the MNDAA attempted to retake territory it had lost in 2009.[[171]](#cite_note-174)[[172]](#cite_note-dvb.no-175) The MNDAA clashed with government troops once again in 2017.[[173]](#cite_note-176)[[174]](#cite_note-177)

The MNDAA resumed its fight against the Tatmadaw in response to the 2021 coup d'état. The MNDAA and Ta'ang National Liberation Army (TNLA) have carried out several joint attacks against the Tatmadaw in Shan State.[[175]](#cite_note-178)

#### Wa State

Main article: [Wa State](/wiki/Wa_State "Wa State")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/UWSA_soldiers_stand_at_attention_during_ceremonies.jpg/250px-UWSA_soldiers_stand_at_attention_during_ceremonies.jpg)](/wiki/File:UWSA_soldiers_stand_at_attention_during_ceremonies.jpg)

UWSA troops standing at attention during a military ceremony.

Following the CPB's internal mutiny on 16 April 1989, ethnic [Wa](/wiki/Wa_people "Wa people") cadres under the command of [Bao Youxiang](/wiki/Bao_Youxiang "Bao Youxiang") announced their split from the party and the founding of the [United Wa State Party](/wiki/United_Wa_State_Party "United Wa State Party") and [United Wa State Army](/wiki/United_Wa_State_Army "United Wa State Army"). The UWSA signed a ceasefire agreement with the [then-ruling military junta](/wiki/State_Law_and_Order_Restoration_Council "State Law and Order Restoration Council") in May 1989 and has since governed parts of Shan State as the autonomous [Wa State](/wiki/Wa_State "Wa State"). The Burmese government has accused their Chinese counterpart of heavily supporting Wa State and the UWSA.[[176]](#cite_note-179)

Foreign involvement
-------------------

Further information: [Foreign involvement in the Myanmar civil war (2021–present)](/wiki/Foreign_involvement_in_the_Myanmar_civil_war_(2021%E2%80%93present) "Foreign involvement in the Myanmar civil war (2021–present)")

### China

The People's Republic of China has long been accused of having a multifaceted role in the conflict, given its close relations with both the Myanmar government and insurgent groups active along the [China–Myanmar border](/wiki/China%E2%80%93Myanmar_border "China–Myanmar border").[[177]](#cite_note-180)

China openly supported the [Communist Party of Burma](/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Burma "Communist Party of Burma") (CPB) and its pursuit of [Mao Zedong Thought](/wiki/Maoism "Maoism") during the 1960s and 1970s.[[178]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith1991235-181)[[179]](#cite_note-182)[[180]](#cite_note-Lintner_1984_p429-183)[[181]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGibson201188-184) After the CPB's armed wing agreed to disarm in 1988, China was accused by Myanmar of continuing to support insurgent groups operating along its border, such as the [United Wa State Army](/wiki/United_Wa_State_Army "United Wa State Army")[[182]](#cite_note-UCDP-185) and [Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army](/wiki/Myanmar_National_Democratic_Alliance_Army "Myanmar National Democratic Alliance Army"), the latter enjoying closer ties to China due to a common [Han Chinese](/wiki/Han_Chinese "Han Chinese") ethnic background.[[183]](#cite_note-ReutersMNDAA-186)

In 2016, China pledged to support Myanmar's peace process by encouraging China-friendly insurgent groups to attend peace talks with the Burmese government and by sending more soldiers to secure its border with Myanmar.[[184]](#cite_note-RFA-187)[[185]](#cite_note-SCMP-188)[[186]](#cite_note-USIP-189) China also offered $3 million [USD](/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar") to fund the negotiations. However, the Burmese government has expressed suspicion over China's involvement in the peace process, due to China's alleged links to the [Northern Alliance](/wiki/Northern_Alliance_(Myanmar) "Northern Alliance (Myanmar)") and the United Wa State Army.[[187]](#cite_note-VOA1-190)

### India

Further information: [Involvement of Northeast Indian insurgents in the Myanmar conflict](/wiki/Involvement_of_Northeast_Indian_insurgents_in_the_Myanmar_conflict "Involvement of Northeast Indian insurgents in the Myanmar conflict")

India and Myanmar share a strategic military relationship due to the overlapping [insurgency in Northeast India](/wiki/Insurgency_in_Northeast_India "Insurgency in Northeast India").[[94]](#cite_note-EconIn16619-97)[[188]](#cite_note-191) India has provided Myanmar's military with training, weapons, and tactical equipment.[[189]](#cite_note-192) The two countries' armies have conducted joint operations against insurgents at their border since the 1990s.[[190]](#cite_note-193) Myanmar has also taken an active role in finding and arresting insurgents that fled from northeast India; in May 2020 Myanmar handed over 22 insurgents, included several top commanders, to Indian authorities.[[191]](#cite_note-194) Similarly, India has been one of the few countries to forcefully repatriate Rohingya refugees back to Myanmar despite global outcry.[[192]](#cite_note-195)

### Pakistan

From 1948 to 1950, Pakistan sent aid to [mujahideen](/wiki/Mujahideen#Myanmar_(Burma) "Mujahideen") in northern Arakan (present-day Rakhine State). In 1950, the Pakistani government warned their Burmese counterparts about their treatment of Muslims. In response, Burmese Prime Minister [U Nu](/wiki/U_Nu "U Nu") immediately sent a Muslim diplomat, [Pe Khin](/wiki/Pe_Khin "Pe Khin"), to negotiate a [memorandum of understanding](/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding "Memorandum of understanding"). Pakistan agreed to cease aid to the mujahideen and arrest members of the group. In 1954, mujahid leader Mir Kassem was arrested by Pakistani authorities, and many of his followers later surrendered to the Burmese government.[[193]](#cite_note-UNu-196)

The [International Crisis Group](/wiki/International_Crisis_Group "International Crisis Group") reported on 14 December 2016 that in interviews with the [Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army](/wiki/Arakan_Rohingya_Salvation_Army "Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army") (ARSA), its leaders claimed to have links to private donors in Saudi Arabia and Pakistan. The ICG also released unconfirmed reports that Rohingya villagers had been "secretly trained" by Afghan and Pakistani fighters.[[194]](#cite_note-Yahoo1-197)[[195]](#cite_note-198) In September 2017, Bangladeshi sources stated that the possibility of cooperation between Pakistan's [Inter-Services Intelligence](/wiki/Inter-Services_Intelligence "Inter-Services Intelligence") (ISI) and ARSA was "extremely high".[[196]](#cite_note-199)

### Russia

In November 2013, delegations from the Burmese and Russian armed forces met in Naypyidaw and agreed to strengthen cooperation between the two, particularly in regards to the exchange of military technology.[[197]](#cite_note-200) Myanmar and Russia signed a military cooperation agreement in June 2016, with Russia promising more arms and training for Myanmar's military.[[198]](#cite_note-201)

### Thailand

Thailand had been a vocal supporter of various insurgent groups in Myanmar, condemning the actions of the then-ruling military juntas and allowing weapons and ammunition to be smuggled through its border.[[199]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcCoy1991[httpsarchiveorgdetailsisbn_9781556521256page370mode2up_370–371]-202) However, in 1995, the Thai government secured its border with Myanmar and stopped all logistical support going through Thailand after they signed a major economic deal with Myanmar.[[116]](#cite_note-Lintner_Thai-119)

### United States

The US [Central Intelligence Agency](/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency "Central Intelligence Agency") (CIA) began aiding [Kuomintang soldiers that fled to Myanmar from China](/wiki/Kuomintang_in_Burma "Kuomintang in Burma") following the advance of [Chinese communist forces](/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") into [Yunnan](/wiki/Yunnan "Yunnan") in 1951. This included *Operation Paper*, which involved supplying them with non-lethal aid via Thailand until 1953, when they airlifted 7,000 soldiers to [Taiwan](/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") and ended the operation.[[200]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGibson201185–90-203)

### Yugoslavia

[Yugoslavia](/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia "Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia") became the primary arms supplier for the Burmese government beginning in 1952, when the Burmese reached out to [Belgrade](/wiki/Belgrade "Belgrade") due to the slow and uneasy support from the United States and the United Kingdom.[[201]](#cite_note-204)[[202]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELintner1999154-205)[[203]](#cite_note-206) The two nations became very close as a result, and the [Yugoslav People's Army](/wiki/Yugoslav_People%27s_Army "Yugoslav People's Army") sent advisors to assist on the frontlines.[[204]](#cite_note-207) The sudden strong relationship between Burma and Yugoslavia prompted concern among the Americans, who worried the Yugoslav support would strengthen Marxist ideology in the government.[[205]](#cite_note-208) Ne Win, who previously sought support solely from the West, was impressed with the speed of the Burmese–Yugoslav cooperation, and travelled to Belgrade in 1953.[[206]](#cite_note-NARA_2253-209)[[207]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEgreteauJagan2013108–109-210)

### Arms suppliers

As of 2019, Myanmar's military is supplied by fourteen [arms companies](/wiki/Arms_industry "Arms industry") from seven countries: China, India, Israel, North Korea, the Philippines, Russia, and Ukraine.[[208]](#cite_note-aa7-211)[[209]](#cite_note-TOI-212)

Despite Serbia signing a non-binding UN resolution calling for the cessation of arms sales to Myanmar following the [2021 Myanmar coup d'état](/wiki/2021_Myanmar_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat "2021 Myanmar coup d'état"), hundreds of Serbian-made 80 mm rockets were sent from Belgrade to Yangon less than a week after the coup.[[210]](#cite_note-213)

Vietnam has also been a vocal supporter of modernisation efforts by Myanmar's military, providing them with ammunition and military hardware.[[211]](#cite_note-214) Burmese military officials have also toured Vietnam to receive military advice from their counterparts in the [People's Army of Vietnam](/wiki/People%27s_Army_of_Vietnam "People's Army of Vietnam").[[212]](#cite_note-215)

### Foreign fighters

[Dave Everett](/wiki/Dave_Everett "Dave Everett") was a member of the Australian [Special Air Service Regiment](/wiki/Special_Air_Service_Regiment "Special Air Service Regiment") before leaving in 1986 and joining the Karen National Liberation Army as a mercenary. Everett fought alongside the KNLA under the alias "Steve" and trained insurgents, helping them improve their marksmanship and teaching them how to use [claymore anti-personnel mines](/wiki/Claymore_mine "Claymore mine"). To fund his time with the KNLA, Everett perpetrated several robberies in Australia with the help of accomplices and took piloting lessons so he could smuggle weapons into Myanmar. Everett returned to Australia a year later in 1987.[[213]](#cite_note-Adshead-216)

Former members of the [British special forces](/wiki/United_Kingdom_Special_Forces "United Kingdom Special Forces"), [Australian special forces](/wiki/Special_forces_of_Australia "Special forces of Australia"), [Green berets](/wiki/US_Army_Special_Forces "US Army Special Forces"), [French Foreign Legion](/wiki/French_Foreign_Legion "French Foreign Legion"), and Russian [Spetsnaz](/wiki/Spetsnaz "Spetsnaz") have also been reported fighting alongside insurgents as recently as 2012.[[214]](#cite_note-Swain-217)

Ceasefire attempts
------------------

Main article: [Ceasefires in Myanmar](/wiki/Ceasefires_in_Myanmar "Ceasefires in Myanmar")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Stop_Civil_War_-_panoramio.jpg/250px-Stop_Civil_War_-_panoramio.jpg)](/wiki/File:Stop_Civil_War_-_panoramio.jpg)

"Stop Civil War in Myanmar" – graffiti on a wall in Yangon, 2014

A number of insurgent groups have negotiated ceasefires and peace agreements with successive governments,[[215]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKramer20099-218) but most have since fallen apart.[[216]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELicklider1995681-219)

Under the new constitutional reforms in 2011, state level and union level ceasefire agreements were made with a number of insurgent groups. Fourteen of the 17 largest rebel factions signed a ceasefire agreement with the new reformed government. All of the 14 signatories wanted negotiations in accordance with the [Panglong Agreement](/wiki/Panglong_Agreement "Panglong Agreement") of 1947, which granted self-determination, a federal system of government (meaning regional autonomy), religious freedom and ethnic minority rights. However, the new constitution, only had a few clauses dedicated to minority rights, and therefore, the government discussed with rebel factions using the new constitution for reference, rather than the Panglong Agreement. There was no inclusive plan or body that represented all the factions, and as a result, in resent, the KNU backed out of the conference and complained the lack of independence for each party within the ethnic bloc.[[217]](#cite_note-Nai,_A._2014-220) However, most of the negotiations between the State Peace Deal Commission and rebel factions were formal and peaceful.[[218]](#cite_note-221)

On 31 March 2015, a draft of the [Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement](/wiki/Nationwide_Ceasefire_Agreement "Nationwide Ceasefire Agreement") (NCA) was finalised between representatives from eight insurgent groups (all part of the Nationwide Ceasefire Coordination Team or NCCT) and the government of Myanmar.[[219]](#cite_note-222) However, only eight of the 15 insurgent groups signed the final agreement on 15 October 2015.[[117]](#cite_note-rfaNCA-120) The signing was witnessed by observers and delegates from the United Nations, the United Kingdom, Norway, Japan and the United States.[[41]](#cite_note-blogs.cfr.org-44)[[42]](#cite_note-Ray_Pagnucco_and_Jennifer_Peters-45) Two other insurgent groups later joined the agreement on 13 February 2018.[[220]](#cite_note-223)[[221]](#cite_note-224)[[222]](#cite_note-225)[[223]](#cite_note-226)

The [Union Peace Conference – 21st Century Panglong](/wiki/Union_Peace_Conference_%E2%80%93_21st_Century_Panglong "Union Peace Conference – 21st Century Panglong") was held from 31 August to 4 September 2016 with several different organisations as representatives, in an attempt to mediate between the government and different insurgent groups. Talks ended without any agreement being reached.[[224]](#cite_note-227) The name of the conference was a reference to the original [Panglong Conference](/wiki/Panglong_Conference "Panglong Conference") held during [British rule](/wiki/British_rule_in_Burma "British rule in Burma") in 1947, which was negotiated between [Aung San](/wiki/Aung_San "Aung San") and ethnic leaders.[[225]](#cite_note-228)

Casualties and displacement of civilians
----------------------------------------

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Mae_La_refugee_camp2.jpg/250px-Mae_La_refugee_camp2.jpg)](/wiki/File:Mae_La_refugee_camp2.jpg)

[Mae La Camp](/wiki/Mae_La_refugee_camp "Mae La refugee camp") in [Tak](/wiki/Tak_Province "Tak Province"), [Thailand](/wiki/Thailand "Thailand"), one of the largest of nine [UNHCR](/wiki/UNHCR "UNHCR") camps in Thailand where over 700,000 refugees, asylum seekers and [stateless](/wiki/Statelessness "Statelessness") persons have fled.[[226]](#cite_note-229)

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Kutupalong_Refugee_Camp_%28Maaz_Hussain-VOA%29.jpg/250px-Kutupalong_Refugee_Camp_%28Maaz_Hussain-VOA%29.jpg)](/wiki/File:Kutupalong_Refugee_Camp_(Maaz_Hussain-VOA).jpg)

[Kutupalong refugee camp](/wiki/Kutupalong_refugee_camp "Kutupalong refugee camp") in Bangladesh, home to nearly 550,000 [Rohingya](/wiki/Rohingya_people "Rohingya people") refugees living in makeshift shelters.

An estimated 130,000 people died in the conflict from 1948 to 2011.[[3]](#cite_note-PERI_20Jul11-5) The [Armed Conflict Location and Event Data Project](/wiki/Armed_Conflict_Location_and_Event_Data_Project "Armed Conflict Location and Event Data Project") (ACLED) has recorded over 53,000 deaths since 2011.[[4]](#cite_note-ACLED_dash-6)

The conflict has also resulted in the displacement of a large number of civilians, many of whom have fled to neighbouring countries such as Thailand, China, India, and Bangladesh. The persecution of [Rohingya people](/wiki/Rohingya_people "Rohingya people") and other ethnic minorities after the 1962 coup led to the expulsion of nearly 300,000 people.[[227]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmith199143–44-230) The UN estimated that between 1996 and 2006, around 1 million people were [internally displaced](/wiki/Internally_displaced_persons "Internally displaced persons") inside Myanmar, over 230,000 of whom remain displaced in the southeast of the country, and 128,000 refugees lived in temporary shelters on the Myanmar–Thailand border.[[228]](#cite_note-ReferenceA-231)[[229]](#cite_note-232) In August 2007, approximately 160,000 refugees fled to nine refugee camps along the Myanmar–Thailand border and the Thai border provinces of [Chiang Mai](/wiki/Chiang_Mai_Province "Chiang Mai Province") and [Ratchaburi](/wiki/Ratchaburi "Ratchaburi"). Approximately 62% of the refugee population consisted of displaced Karen people. Humanitarian organisations such as [Doctors Without Borders](/wiki/Doctors_Without_Borders "Doctors Without Borders") have since sent workers and medical support to the refugees.[[230]](#cite_note-233) As of 2014[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Myanmar_conflict&action=edit), an estimated 600,000 to 1 million people remain internally displaced in Myanmar.[[231]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHampton2014-234)[*[page needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources")*]

Over the course of the conflict, government officials in Myanmar have been accused of forcefully removing civilians living in conflict areas and confiscating their property, to repurpose them [for commercial, industrial, and military projects](/wiki/Development-induced_displacement "Development-induced displacement").[[228]](#cite_note-ReferenceA-231)[[232]](#cite_note-oxfordburmaalliance.org-235)[[233]](#cite_note-236)

In Rakhine State, there were around 75,000 internally displaced [Rohingyas](/wiki/Rohingya_people "Rohingya people") in 2012, according to Refugee International.[[234]](#cite_note-237) UNICEF has reported that living conditions in Rohingya refugee camps in Rakhine State are "wholly inadequate" and lacks access to basic services.[[235]](#cite_note-238) In October 2017, there were an estimated 947,000 [Rohingya refugees in Bangladesh](/wiki/Rohingya_refugees_in_Bangladesh "Rohingya refugees in Bangladesh").[[236]](#cite_note-239) The Rohingya people have been described by the United Nations as "among the world's least wanted" and "one of the world's most persecuted minorities."[[237]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTETanIslam2007342-240)

The Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre reports a total of 401,000 people internally displaced in Myanmar as of 2018, owing both to man-made and natural disasters as well as conflict and violence.[[98]](#cite_note-:0-101) This figure includes IDPs across the country, with 131,000 in Rakhine State, 97,000 in Kachin State, 50,000 in Kayin State, 40,000 in Tanintharyi Region, 27,000 in Karenni State, 22,000 in Bago Region, 18,000 in Mon State, 15,000 in Shan State and 1,300 in Chin State.[[238]](#cite_note-241) Of these total displacements, IDMC estimates that approximately 42,000 people were newly displaced in 2018 by conflict and violence.[[98]](#cite_note-:0-101) Compared to 2017, the rate of new displacements was lower in Rakhine State but higher in Kachin State and northern Shan State, which together saw approximately 36,000 people displaced.[[98]](#cite_note-:0-101)

The Global Camp Coordination and Camp Management Cluster (CCCM) estimated in 2019 that at least 941,000 people in Myanmar were in need of humanitarian assistance, with over 128,000 people living in IDP camps in Rakhine State and over 105,000 people displaced in Kachin State and northern Shan State.[[239]](#cite_note-:1-242) While many displacements last only for the duration of active fighting, protracted displacement is evidenced by camps in Kachin State, Rakhine State, and Shan State.[[98]](#cite_note-:0-101) Living situations in these camps are often overcrowded with inadequate shelter, sanitation, healthcare, food, and education.[[240]](#cite_note-:2-243) In total, approximately 35 per cent of IDPs in Myanmar are estimated to live in non-government controlled areas that have limited if not wholly restricted access as of November 2019, complicating relief efforts both for international and local organisations.[[239]](#cite_note-:1-242)

Human rights violations
-----------------------

Main article: [Human rights in Myanmar](/wiki/Human_rights_in_Myanmar "Human rights in Myanmar")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/BGP_officer_near_a_burnt_down_house_in_Rakhine_State.jpg/250px-BGP_officer_near_a_burnt_down_house_in_Rakhine_State.jpg)](/wiki/File:BGP_officer_near_a_burnt_down_house_in_Rakhine_State.jpg)

A member of Myanmar's [Border Guard Police](/wiki/Border_Guard_Police "Border Guard Police") inspects a house burnt down in an arson attack.

The Tatmadaw have been accused of using "[scorched earth](/wiki/Scorched_earth "Scorched earth")" tactics against civilians. The accusations include burning down entire villages, planting [landmines](/wiki/Landmines "Landmines"), using civilians as slave labour, using civilians as minesweepers, and using civilians as sex slaves.[[114]](#cite_note-NYFree-117) According to a report by legal firm [DLA Piper](/wiki/DLA_Piper "DLA Piper"), whose report was presented to the [United Nations Security Council](/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council"), these tactics have been identified as [ethnic cleansing](/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing "Ethnic cleansing").[[115]](#cite_note-DLApiper-118)

Both sides have been accused of using [landmines](/wiki/Landmines "Landmines"), which have caused hundreds of accidental civilian injuries and deaths. The KNU has been accused of planting landmines in rural areas, most of which have not been disarmed. The KNU claims that landmines are vital for repelling government troops because they "discourage [the Tatmadaw] from attacking civilians". However, the majority of those who fall victim to landmines planted by the KNU are local civilians and not government soldiers.[[241]](#cite_note-244) Victims of landmines must travel to the Myanmar–Thailand border to seek treatment, as local hospitals and facilities lack proper equipment and funding.[[242]](#cite_note-Nazaryan_10715-245)

Both sides have also been accused of using thousands of [child soldiers](/wiki/Child_soldiers "Child soldiers"), despite the fact that the government of Myanmar and seven insurgent groups signed an agreement with [UNICEF](/wiki/UNICEF "UNICEF") in 2012, promising not to exploit children for military and political gains. The [International Labour Organization](/wiki/International_Labour_Organization "International Labour Organization") (ILO) has accused both sides of continuing to use child soldiers in violation of the agreement. According to the ILO, the Tatmadaw has discharged hundreds of child soldiers since 2012; however, the ILO also estimated that at least 340 child soldiers had been recruited by the Tatmadaw between 2013 and 2014.[[243]](#cite_note-246) Meanwhile, insurgent groups such as the MNDAA, SSA-S, and TNLA have reportedly [press-ganged](/wiki/Impressment "Impressment") minors into their armies.[[244]](#cite_note-247)

One of the most notable cases in which child soldiers were used in Myanmar was that of twins [Johnny and Luther Htoo](/wiki/Johnny_and_Luther_Htoo "Johnny and Luther Htoo"), the leaders of [God's Army](/wiki/God%27s_Army_(revolutionary_group) "God's Army (revolutionary group)"), a former rebel faction. When God's Army was formed in 1997, the pair were just ten-years-old.[[245]](#cite_note-248)

On 25 July 2022, the [Human Rights Watch](/wiki/Human_Rights_Watch "Human Rights Watch") reported that Myanmar's military junta sentenced four men to death after closed trials that fell far short of international standards. The men put to death were [Phyo Zeya Thaw](/wiki/Zayar_Thaw "Zayar Thaw"), [Kyaw Min Yu](/wiki/Kyaw_Min_Yu "Kyaw Min Yu"), Hla Myo Aung and Aung Thura Zaw. These executions followed grossly unjust and politically motivated military trials.[[246]](#cite_note-249)

Organised crime
---------------

As the conflict has dragged on, transnational criminal groups have continued to operate and expand in Myanmar. In the months following the February 2021 coup, the [United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime](/wiki/United_Nations_Office_on_Drugs_and_Crime "United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime")’s representative for Southeast Asia and the Pacific noted that "the opium economy is really a poverty economy", and raised concerns that the likely economic impacts of the coup would lead people in poor and poverty-stricken areas to look to the opium economy to make money: "Probably 12 months out, 18 months out, we’re going to be looking at an expansion unless past history is wrong. There’s a cycle of this happening in the country over its history".[[247]](#cite_note-250)

According to UNODC's Myanmar Opium Survey 2024, the area under opium poppy cultivation in Myanmar expanded each year from 2020 to 2022. There was a 4% decrease in area under cultivation in 2023, but according to UNODC, the reasons for this decline are varied and may relate to broader dynamics of the regional opiate economy as well as the ongoing armed conflict, and it remains unclear if this represents a new chapter in illicit cultivation.[[248]](#cite_note-:3-251) Despite the modest drop in the area of cultivation, according to UNODC: "The amount of opium produced in Myanmar remains close to the highest levels we have seen since we first measured it more than 20 years ago … As conflict dynamics in the country remain intense and the global supply chains adjust to the ban in Afghanistan, we see significant risk of a further expansion over the coming years."[[248]](#cite_note-:3-251)

After the coup, sites housing illegal online gambling and cyber-enabled fraud operations expanded. This included the [Yatai New City](/wiki/Yatai_New_City "Yatai New City") complex in Shwe Kokko, which had been frozen and placed under investigation by the NLD government, but restarted and expanded significantly following the coup.[[249]](#cite_note-252) In other areas, including many controlled by armed groups allied with the junta, cybercrime sites proliferated.

International responses
-----------------------

Further information: [International reactions to the Rohingya genocide](/wiki/International_reactions_to_the_Rohingya_genocide "International reactions to the Rohingya genocide") and [Foreign involvement in the Myanmar civil war (2021–present)](/wiki/Foreign_involvement_in_the_Myanmar_civil_war_(2021%E2%80%93present) "Foreign involvement in the Myanmar civil war (2021–present)")

Since 1991, the [UN General Assembly](/wiki/UN_General_Assembly "UN General Assembly") has adopted twenty-five different resolutions regarding Myanmar's government, condemning previous military juntas for their systematic violations of human rights and lack of political freedom.[[250]](#cite_note-ALTUN-253) In 2009 they urged the then [ruling junta](/wiki/State_Peace_and_Development_Council "State Peace and Development Council") to take urgent measures to end violations of international human rights and humanitarian laws in the country.[[251]](#cite_note-254)

Reports of human rights abuses committed by the military and local paramilitaries prompted the UN Human Rights Council to launch an independent international fact-finding mission in March 2017, with which Myanmar's government failed to cooperate.[[252]](#cite_note-255) The mission's report (A/HRC/39/64) released in September 2018 highlighted "clear patterns" of serious human rights abuses and violations of international humanitarian law in Kachin State, Rakhine State, and Shan State since 2011. The Tatmadaw are accused of deliberate and systematic targeting of civilians, sexual violence, discriminatory rhetoric against minorities, and impunity for its soldiers.[[240]](#cite_note-:2-243)

Eyewitness testimony alleged that in Rakhine State, "clearance operations" by the Tatmadaw amounted to planned and deliberate mass killings in at least 54 locations.[[240]](#cite_note-:2-243) Hundreds and perhaps thousands of Rohingya women and girls were reported to have been raped, including in mass gang rapes, and at least 392 Rohingya villages were burned to the ground.[[240]](#cite_note-:2-243) The report also highlighted the conviction of [Wa Lone](/wiki/Wa_Lone "Wa Lone") and [Kyaw Soe Oo](/wiki/Kyaw_Soe_Oo "Kyaw Soe Oo"), two Reuters reporters who had exposed the military's [extrajudicial killing of ten Rohingya men](/wiki/Inn_Din_massacre "Inn Din massacre") and were subsequently imprisoned; the journalists have since been released and awarded a 2019 [Pulitzer Prize](/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize "Pulitzer Prize") for their reporting.[[253]](#cite_note-256)

In addition to violence against Rohingya communities, the report noted Tatamadaw abuses against ethnic Rakhine, including forced labour, sexual violence, forced evictions, and killings. It also highlighted crimes committed by insurgent groups in Kachin State, Rakhine State, and Shan State, including arson, extortion, destruction of property, forced labour, rape, murder, and forced disappearances.[[254]](#cite_note-257) The mission called for an investigation into and the prosecution of military leaders, in particular commander-in-chief Senior General [Min Aung Hlaing](/wiki/Min_Aung_Hlaing "Min Aung Hlaing"), in the [International Criminal Court](/wiki/International_Criminal_Court "International Criminal Court") (ICC) for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes.

[The Gambia](/wiki/The_Gambia "The Gambia") [filed a lawsuit](/wiki/Rohingya_genocide_case "Rohingya genocide case") against Myanmar in the [International Court of Justice](/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice "International Court of Justice") on 11 November 2019. Myanmar State Counsellor Aung San Suu Kyi defended her country's military generals against accusations of genocide in public hearings in December 2019.[[255]](#cite_note-258)
