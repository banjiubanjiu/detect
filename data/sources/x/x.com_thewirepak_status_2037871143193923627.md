Assessment: Iran War 
By Ali K. Chishti 
The 2026 Iran War, launched by the US and Israel on February 28 via Operation Epic Fury, has expanded into a multi-front conflict heavily impacting the Gulf. Initial waves of nearly 900 strikes targeted Iranian nuclear sites, missile infrastructure, air defenses, navy, and leadership, resulting in the death of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Iran’s retaliation has focused on a disruption strategy against Gulf states Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE firing thousands of missiles and drones at US bases, airports, energy facilities (e.g., Qatar’s Ras Laffan LNG, Saudi refineries, UAE infrastructure), and civilian areas.

Iran’s approach treats the Gulf as a primary theater to impose economic pain, pressure Washington’s allies, and deter deeper involvement. By attacking oil/gas sites, ports, and data centers while imposing a selective “toll” regime on the Strait of Hormuz (attacking non-compliant ships, blocking US/Israeli/allied traffic), Tehran aims to spike global oil prices, disrupt 20% of world supply, and force negotiations on its terms including reparations and recognition of Iranian influence over the strait. Gulf states have faced the brunt: hundreds of intercepts required, civilian casualties, damaged refineries/LNG capacity, and economic strain, pushing some (e.g., UAE, Bahrain) toward defensive multinational maritime efforts.

Military balance: US-Israel maintain air/naval superiority, degrading ~70-90% of Iran’s long-range missile/drone launch capacity and much of its navy. Iranian barrages have decreased in volume but persist asymmetrically via proxies and remaining assets. US forces in the Gulf exceed 50,000 with carriers and reinforcements; Gulf states contribute defensively but show growing frustration.

Diplomacy: Pakistan has emerged as a key indirect mediator, relaying a US 15-point proposal (dismantle nuclear program, curb missiles/proxies) to Iran, which has countered with demands for reparations and Hormuz control. Iran’s president has praised Pakistan’s efforts, but gaps remain wide; Turkey and Egypt offer parallel support. No breakthrough yet, with Trump signaling possible further strikes on energy targets if Hormuz stays disrupted.

Outlook: US-Israel hold conventional edge and have severely weakened Iran’s strategic tools, yet Iran’s Gulf disruption strategy sustains leverage through economic spillover. Regime resilience persists amid mobilization claims, but internal pressures and degraded capabilities limit endurance. A negotiated settlement via Pakistani channels appears viable if Iran accepts program limits; otherwise, risks of escalated Gulf strikes, Hormuz confrontation, or limited ground actions grow. The war has already redrawn regional security, elevated Gulf vulnerabilities, and strained global energy markets.
