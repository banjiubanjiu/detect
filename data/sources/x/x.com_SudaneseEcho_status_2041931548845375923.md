Investigation Reveals Evidence of UAE-Led Support Network via Ethiopia Supplying the Rapid Support Militia (Janjaweed), According to Yale Analysis

An exclusive investigation published by Middle East Eye, authored by journalists Oscar Rickett and Zecharias Zelalem, and based on satellite imagery analysis conducted by the Humanitarian Research Lab at the Yale School of Public Health, reveals the existence of an organized military support network for the Rapid Support Militia (Janjaweed), operating from within an Ethiopian military base in the city of Assosa, in the Benishangul-Gumuz region.

The investigation presents visual evidence and open-source analysis covering the period from December 2025 to March 2026, showing that the base has been transformed into a fully functioning logistical hub supporting militia operations. This includes vehicle preparation, fuel supply, maintenance, and accommodation, all consistent with sustained, high-tempo military support activity.

Satellite imagery shows repeated movement of vehicle transporters entering and exiting the base, carrying “technical” vehicles. Approximately 200 such vehicles were observed in February alone, many of which were modified with mounts capable of supporting heavy machine guns. The site also includes tent structures capable of housing up to 150 personnel, along with organized distribution of supplies.

In a key statement to Middle East Eye, the Executive Director of the Humanitarian Research Lab at Yale, Nathaniel Raymond, said:

“Since the fall of last year there have been allegations that Ethiopia has allowed the RSF to base its attacks on Blue Nile from Ethiopia. This report is the first visual evidence that those allegations are true. In fact, it is even worse than first feared. Not only are the Ethiopians assisting the RSF, they are doing it from an actual Ethiopian army base.”

Multiple sources cited in the investigation, including current and former Ethiopian military officials, analysts, a European diplomat, and a former advisor to Ethiopia’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs, confirmed that the Assosa base is being used by the militia and that its location had been kept under tight secrecy.

A former Ethiopian government advisor told the outlet:

“many in Ethiopia’s foreign ministry and elsewhere believe the UAE has been calling the shots in Ethiopia in regard to the Sudan government, RSF and Eritrea over the port of Assab for at least two years”

The investigation further highlights the central role of the United Arab Emirates in facilitating Ethiopia’s involvement in the Sudanese war, despite ongoing denials. The evidence points to direct logistical links between supply routes and activities inside the base.

Open-source videos show vehicle carriers transporting white and sand-colored “technical” vehicles from the port of Berbera in Somaliland, which hosts a UAE-operated base, into Ethiopian territory. These same types of vehicles later appear in combat footage inside Sudan.

Flight tracking data also reveals multiple cargo flights linked to the UAE traveling from Abu Dhabi to airports in Ethiopia, including Bahir Dar, located roughly 300 kilometers from Assosa. Aircraft and helicopters were also observed within the base during February and March.

The vehicles prepared at the base closely match those later documented in combat operations in Sudan, particularly in Blue Nile State and in the border town of Kurmuk, located approximately 100 kilometers from Assosa. Kurmuk witnessed intense fighting that ended with the militia and its allies taking control of the town in late March.

Providing broader geopolitical context, North Africa and political economy analyst Jalel Harchaoui told Middle East Eye:

“Addis Ababa will stick with the UAE. The Emiratis are therefore focusing military operations on Ethiopian territory, preparing a major offensive as Saudi actions have disrupted other staging areas.”

Researcher on the Horn of Africa, Goitom Gebreluel, explained that Ethiopia’s involvement is shaped by both geopolitical alignments and internal security considerations. He noted that regional alliances link Ethiopia’s strategic interests with those of the UAE, and by extension with the Rapid Support Militia (Janjaweed), alongside ongoing tensions with Sudanese leadership.

The investigation also cites remarks by Emirati academic Abdulkhaleq Abdulla, who criticized what he described as the “targeting” of the UAE over allegations of support for the militia. His comments come at a time when evidence continues to accumulate. Many observers interpret his remarks as an implicit acknowledgment of the nature of the relationship, particularly given his proximity to the country’s decision-making circles, which adds weight to the implications of his statement.

The report situates these developments within the broader context of the Sudanese war, which began in April 2023 and has resulted in hundreds of thousands of deaths and the displacement of over 11 million people, making it one of the largest humanitarian crises in the world.

It also references findings from an international fact-finding mission that concluded the Rapid Support Militia (Janjaweed) committed acts of genocide in Darfur, with civilians reporting killings, sexual violence, and severe abuses while fleeing conflict zones.

In conclusion, the Yale Humanitarian Research Lab report describes Assosa as a “key logistical node” supporting activities consistent with direct military assistance. The base is equipped to provide supply, refueling, vehicle maintenance, and accommodation for fighters. The observed activity does not resemble that of other Ethiopian military bases in the region.

This investigation, supported by visual evidence and rigorous analysis, provides a clearer picture of the scale and structure of external involvement in Sudan’s war, transforming long-standing allegations into documented and verifiable findings that place increasing pressure on the international community to respond.
